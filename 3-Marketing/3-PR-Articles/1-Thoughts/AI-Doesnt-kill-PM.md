# AI Didn't Kill Product Managers — But It's Forcing All of Us to Level Up

In March 2026, Lenny Rachitsky published data that should end the "AI is replacing PMs" panic once and for all. Open PM roles at tech companies hit [7,300+ globally](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/state-of-the-product-job-market-in-ee9) — the highest level since 2022, up 75% from the early 2023 trough. AI-specific PM roles exploded by 465%. Meanwhile, design roles flatlined. The market isn't shrinking. Market is interested in people who decide *what* to build.

This shouldn't surprise anyone who understands Jevons paradox.

## The Jevons paradox is playing out in real time

In 1865, economist William Stanley Jevons observed something counterintuitive: as steam engines became more efficient, coal consumption didn't fall — it skyrocketed. Efficiency made coal useful in more contexts, so total demand exploded. Economists call this Jevons paradox, and we're watching it play out with AI right now.

Code is getting radically cheaper to produce. But it's not just code. Writing product specs, analyzing user data, creating mockups and prototypes, building landing pages, designing email sequences — practically everything that constitutes a product manager's daily work is becoming near-free. 

And that's making thousands of previously unjustifiable projects suddenly viable. Every internal tool that "wasn't worth the engineering time" is now being built. Every hypothesis that "we'll test next quarter" can be tested today. Every landing page that needed a designer and a developer can now be shipped by one person before lunch.

Efficiency doesn't shrink demand. It unleashes it. More software being built means more decisions about *what* to build, *for whom*, and *why*. That's product management. The demand isn't going away — it's accelerating.

## Every role is moving up one level of abstraction

PMs are no longer writing user stories and shuffling Jira tickets. The job is now navigating markets, segments, value propositions, business models, channels, and partnerships. Developers aren't writing code — they own architecture and deployment pipelines. Designers aren't drawing mockups — they're building design systems that let AI-assisted teams ship consistent interfaces 100x faster.

The work didn't vanish — it moved up. We're heading toward an explosion of well-made decisions. But that requires people who can actually make them.

## Not replacement — redistribution. But everyone has to retrain.

The [World Economic Forum's Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/) projects that by 2030, 92 million jobs will be displaced — but 170 million new ones will be created, a net gain of 78 million. Five typists became one PC operator, but the total number of knowledge workers grew by orders of magnitude. The pie is getting bigger.

What's changing is the nature of PM work, not the demand for it. The tasks that used to fill your calendar — writing tickets, scheduling standups, aligning stakeholders, grooming backlogs — are now handled faster and cheaper by AI. That frees you up for the work that actually drives business outcomes: choosing the right customer segment, identifying a job-to-be-done worth solving, designing a business model with real unit economics.

But "frees you up" is the optimistic framing. If those strategic skills aren't in your toolkit yet, you need to build them. Developers are learning product thinking. Designers are learning data analysis. PMs are learning to operate AI agents and ship code. The roles aren't disappearing — they're expanding, and the people in them need to expand with them.

## What this means for founders

If you're building a startup in 2026, the playbook has changed. You don't need a ten-person team to test a product hypothesis — you need one person with business judgment and an AI agent. Build first, then research. Ship an MVP, show it to real users, iterate 50 times in a month instead of five times in a year.

But this only works if the person steering knows where to point the ship. AI is a jet engine. Without navigation, it just gets you lost faster.

Marc Andreessen used to say "software is eating the world." Now something is eating software. But the people who understand why customers buy and how to monetize it — they win massively.
