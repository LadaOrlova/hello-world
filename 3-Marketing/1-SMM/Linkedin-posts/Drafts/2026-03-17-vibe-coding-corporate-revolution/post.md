# Vibe Coding Corporate Revolution

You're sitting in a meeting right now thinking: "I could have built 3 prototypes by now."
Here's what to do with that feeling.

That frustration you feel? It's not impatience. It's a signal.

You've tasted what's possible with AI coding tools — shipping a working prototype in hours instead of weeks. And now every status update, every alignment meeting, every "let's circle back" feels like watching paint dry in slow motion.

I've seen this movie before. It's exactly how the product management revolution started.

Someone inside a company learned about product thinking. Came back to work and said: "We can do this better." They didn't wait for a mandate from above. They just started doing it differently. Enough of those people created a tipping point — and entire industries transformed from the bottom up.

The same thing is happening right now with vibe coding and AI agents. McKinsey found that employees are 3x ahead of leadership in AI adoption. The awakening is already underway.

So here's the playbook if you're that person:

→ Go to your boss and say: "Give me one problem and full autonomy. I'll show you a working solution this week. You take the credit upstairs."

That's it. Don't ask for permission to transform the company. Don't write a proposal. Build something real and put it on the table. When leadership sees the output, they won't ask how you did it — they'll ask what else you can do.

This is how people become leaders. Show results. Capture more responsibility. Repeat.

Now — the honest part. Yes, you might burn out. When AI removes the speed limit on execution, the only bottleneck left is your own judgment. That's a different kind of exhaustion — not overwork, but decision fatigue from shipping faster than you've ever shipped before.

But here's the reframe: it's better to burn bright building something real than to slowly smolder through another year of meetings about meetings.

The people who feel that meeting frustration aren't broken. They're the ones who'll transform their companies from the inside — just like the product revolution before them.

What's the moment you realized you couldn't go back to the old speed?
