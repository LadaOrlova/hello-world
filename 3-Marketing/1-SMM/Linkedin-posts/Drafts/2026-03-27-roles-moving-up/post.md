# Every Role Is Moving Up One Level of Abstraction

Every role in tech is about to move up one level of abstraction.
Here's what that actually means for your career in the next 12 months.

Developers are no longer writing code. They own architecture and deployment processes — making sure the right code ships correctly. Analysts are no longer solving one-off tasks. They're responsible for ensuring the ENTIRE company makes decisions with accurate, up-to-date data — as if a whole analytics department were working, not one person.

And PMs? You're not writing user stories and moving Jira cards anymore. Your job is now markets, segments, value propositions, business models, channels, and partnerships. The role is moving to the business level.

This is already happening in concrete ways.

A year ago, an AI legal product gave advice on documents. Today, it's a digital lawyer that handles ALL of a company's legal tasks for $200/month. The job "review this document" disappeared as a class — the same way "insert a CD into a player" disappeared. Nobody noticed until it was gone.

Same pattern with software development. The entire job graph of "find a developer → hire → negotiate salary → onboard → assign tasks → review code → manage performance" has collapsed. Tools like Claude Code moved up one level — they're digital developers. Not assistants. Not copilots. Developers.

What this means at scale is staggering. We're heading toward an explosion of well-made decisions. For that to happen, hundreds of millions of people need to stop shuffling papers and start actually thinking — which segments do we serve, what value do we deliver, which channels work, are we efficient.

Here's the thing most people miss: the barrier isn't financial. The tools cost $20/month. The barrier is psychological. It's the willingness to let go of process work that felt like productivity and step into strategic work that feels uncomfortable.

"But won't this cause mass layoffs?" History says no. Five typists became one operator — but the total number of knowledge workers grew by orders of magnitude. We're watching redistribution, not elimination. The pie is getting bigger.

The real question is whether you'll move up with your role — or get replaced by someone who did.

What level of abstraction is YOUR role moving toward?
