## 1. Hook Patterns

MtP hooks fall into three templates. Average hook length: **40-70 words** (2-3 sentences before the first subheading or thesis statement).

### Template A — Tension hook (most common)
Open with two opposing realities the reader lives in right now, then promise resolution.

> *"If you work in product leadership right now, you are probably living in two realities. First, the AI everywhere reality... Second, the operational reality."* — 2026 AI strategy guide

### Template B — Anecdote hook
A short story (personal or borrowed) that acts as a metaphor for the article's argument.

> *"A factory's critical machine breaks down... A veteran technician studies the machine, taps once with a small hammer..."* — Building products in Africa

### Template C — Data hook
Lead with a striking statistic, then immediately reframe what it means for PMs.

> *"MIT published a report... US business has so far invested between $35 billion and $40 billion in generative AI with very little to show for it, with 95% of generative AI pilots yielding no business impact."* — Why most AI products fail

**Key finding:** The hook almost never starts with "I". Even first-person articles begin with an external fact, tension, or story before the author enters.

---

## 2. Transition Style

MtP articles use three transition devices between sections:

### a) Bridge sentence before the subheading
A 1-sentence paragraph that links the previous section's takeaway to what comes next:
> *"This emphasis on figuring out the right problems to solve brings me to one of the most critical principles in product development."*

### b) Direct "let's" / imperative pivot
> *"Let's break it down."*
> *"But let's start with what the data actually says."*

### c) Reframe + contrast
The author restates a common belief, then pivots:
> *"You might think this is just a gaming story. It is not."*
> *"Founders landing at #9 might seem underwhelming, but it actually represents a 69% increase year-over-year."*

**Key finding:** Sections almost never start cold. There is always either a bridge sentence at the end of the previous section or a reframe in the first sentence of the new section.

---

## 3. Paragraph Rhythm

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Average sentences per paragraph | 2-3 |
| Max sentences per paragraph | 4-5 (rare, usually in case study sections) |
| 1-sentence paragraphs per article | 3-5 |
| 1-sentence paragraph purpose | Emphasis, punchline, or pace reset |

### Examples of 1-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis:
- *"It obviously is."* (after rhetorical setup)
- *"The key is ownership."*
- *"Launch is the start of the journey, not the finish line."*

**Key finding:** MtP writers use a **long-short-long rhythm**. A 3-sentence paragraph, then a 1-sentence punch, then back to 2-3 sentences. This creates a spoken, rhythmic feel without becoming choppy.

### Summary
- **Typical practical article:** 1-3 data points per 1000 words, 0-1 expert quotes per 1000 words.
- **Data-driven news piece** (MIT report): much higher density, but this is an outlier format.
- **Expert quotes** are short (1-2 sentences) and always attributed with name + title. Never block-quoted walls of text.

**Key finding:** MtP practical articles are light on data. They prefer **one strong number** over a barrage of stats. When data appears, it is immediately interpreted ("what this means for you").

## 5. Close Patterns

Three closing patterns observed:

### Pattern A — Reframe + inspirational close (most common)
Restate the article's core tension, then resolve it with a principle:
> *"The best AI launches I've seen didn't just ship quickly — they shipped deliberately... precision — not speed — is what makes your product unforgettable."*

### Pattern B — Takeaway list + methodology note
Bullet-point "key takeaways for product people" followed by a credibility note:
> *"Key takeaways from the report for product people are: [bullets]... The report's authors say it is based on a multi-method research design."*

### Pattern C — Open question / "your move"
End with what the reader should decide, not what they should think:
> *"For product managers reading this, the message is clear: you're well-positioned with the skills you have. The question is what you want to do next."*

**Key finding:** MtP closings are short (2-4 sentences). They never introduce new arguments. They almost always include a forward-looking statement ("what to do next" / "what will remain"). There is no hard CTA like "subscribe" or "download" — the CTA is implicit ("try this").

## 6. Tone Markers

### Contractions
Used freely and consistently: "you're", "didn't", "don't", "it's", "won't", "there's". Avoidance of contractions would sound too formal for MtP.

### Person
- **"You" (2nd person)** dominates in advice sections: "your job as a product leader is not just to..."
- **"I" (1st person)** appears in guest posts with personal experience: "I have seen ideas with genuine potential fall flat."
- **"We"** is rare — used only when speaking for the PM community generically.

### Rhetorical questions
- Used sparingly: **1-3 per article** in practical pieces.
- Often appear in subheadings: *"How do you build an AI roadmap that survives scrutiny from your CFO and your future self?"*
- Sometimes used as a diagnostic tool within the body: *"Can a heuristic or rule-based system solve this just as well?"*

### Colloquialisms
Moderate. Examples: "fall flat", "very little to show for it", "borderline insane", "roars back to life", "the AI boat". MtP allows vivid, conversational phrases but avoids slang.

### Sentence starters
MtP articles frequently start sentences with "But", "And", "So" — a deliberate informality marker.

---

## 7. What MtP Articles NEVER Do

Based on analysis of 5 articles:

1. **Never open with a definition.** No article begins with "Product management is..." or "AI is defined as..." They open with tension, story, or data.

2. **Never use passive hedging.** No "it could be argued that" or "one might consider." They state positions directly.

3. **Never dump uninterpreted data.** Every statistic is immediately followed by "what this means" or "why this matters."

4. **Never use academic citations.** No "(Smith et al., 2024)". Sources are linked inline or mentioned by name naturally: "MIT published a report..."

5. **Never preach or lecture.** Even authoritative articles maintain a peer tone. The author is a fellow PM, not a professor.

6. **Never use long introductions.** The hook is 2-3 sentences max before the article gets to substance. There is no "in this article, we will explore..." meta-narration.

7. **Never end without forward momentum.** No article ends on a summary alone — there is always a "what now" or "what next" nudge.

8. **Never use jargon without context.** When a term like "shadow AI" or "COGS" appears, it is either immediately explained or assumed common PM knowledge.

---

## Quick-Reference Checklist for the Writer

- [ ] Hook: 40-70 words. Tension, anecdote, or data — never a definition or "I".
- [ ] First subheading by word 80-120.
- [ ] Paragraph rhythm: 2-3 sentences default, with 3-5 one-sentence paragraphs for punch.
- [ ] Transitions: always bridge or reframe between sections — no cold starts.
- [ ] Data: 1-3 strong numbers per 1000 words. Interpret each one immediately.
- [ ] Quotes: 0-2 per article. Short, attributed, never block-quoted.
- [ ] Contractions: use them. "You're", "don't", "it's" are standard.
- [ ] Sentence starters: "But", "And", "So" are welcome.
- [ ] Rhetorical questions: 1-3 max. Best in subheadings or as diagnostic prompts.
- [ ] Closing: 2-4 sentences. Reframe + "what to do next". No new arguments.
- [ ] Never: definitions as openers, passive hedging, academic tone, uninterpreted data, meta-narration ("in this article we will...").
