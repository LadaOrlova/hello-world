# Consumer Research: Parents of Teenagers (13-18) -- Technology & AI Education

**Methodology:** AJTBD (Advanced Jobs To Be Done)
**Date:** 2026-03-28
**Data sources:** Pew Research (Feb 2026), Common Sense Media (2025), Zety survey (2025), Learner.com survey (2025), PwC Back-to-School report (2025), WEF Future of Jobs 2025, market pricing analysis of iD Tech / NextGen Bootcamp / CodaKid / Code.org

---

## Part 1: Macro Context -- The Fear Landscape

### 1.1 The Scale of Parental Anxiety

The data is unambiguous: parents are deeply anxious about AI's impact on their children's futures.

- **97%** of parents fear AI could disrupt or replace their child's career within the next decade (Zety survey, 2025)
- **53%** of parents are concerned AI will narrow their children's job prospects (Common Sense Media)
- **62%** of parents discussed "AI and the future of work" with their teens in just the past two weeks (Common Sense Media)
- **80%** of teens themselves say they're worried about AI's risks (Common Sense Media)
- **47%** of children expressed worry for their economic futures due to AI advancements

When parents describe their feelings about the AI-driven future, the words used most frequently were: **concerned, cautious, uncertain, worried.**

### 1.2 The School Trust Crisis

Parents don't trust schools to solve this:

- **1 in 3 parents** (36%) aren't confident schools are preparing students for AI-era jobs
- **96%** of elementary-school families either don't know about any school AI policy or explicitly say their school hasn't communicated anything
- Only **26%** of parents think their child's school has rules for how ChatGPT can be used
- **50%** of parents are unaware of their school's policy regarding AI tools

### 1.3 The Awareness Gap

Parents dramatically underestimate their teen's AI usage:

- **64%** of teens use AI chatbots, including ~30% daily (Pew Research, Feb 2026)
- **54%** of teens use AI for schoolwork, but only **26%** of parents know about it
- **72%** of teens have used AI companions at least once
- **~1/3** of teens use AI for romantic interactions, emotional support, and friendship
- Only **51%** of parents say their teen uses chatbots (vs. 64% actual usage)

### 1.4 What Parents Fear Most (Ranked)

1. **Career displacement** -- 97% fear disruption within a decade
2. **Over-dependence on AI for schoolwork** -- 66% cite this as a major concern
3. **Cheating** -- 60% major concern (59% of teens confirm cheating is "a regular feature of student life")
4. **Inappropriate content** -- 60% major concern (sexual, violent, racist material)
5. **Data misuse** -- 84% worry about AI misusing kids' data
6. **Addiction to virtual relationships** -- teens are more worried about this than parents
7. **Loss of basic skills** -- nearly 2/3 believe AI will undermine children's basic skill development

## Part 3: Parent Segments by AJTBD

---

### SEGMENT A: "Future-Proof My Child's Career"

**Size estimate:** Largest segment (~35-40% of the market)

**Core Job:**
When **I see headlines about AI replacing jobs and my teen has no technical skills**, I want **to enroll them in a program that teaches them to work WITH AI, not be replaced BY it**, so that **they have a viable career path regardless of how the economy changes.**

**Trigger events:**
- Reading news about AI layoffs or automation
- Conversations with other parents at work or school events
- Their own workplace adopting AI tools (firsthand disruption)
- The 97% stat circulating on social media
- Teen asking "what's the point of studying X if AI can do it?"

**Demographic profile:**
- Parents aged 38-52
- Often white-collar professionals themselves
- College-educated (62% with BA+ support schools teaching AI skills)
- Household income $80K-$180K
- Often in industries already affected by AI (marketing, finance, legal, tech-adjacent)

**Decision criteria (ranked):**
1. **Outcome credibility** -- "What will my kid actually be able to DO after this?"
2. **Curriculum relevance** -- Must include AI/ML, not just basic coding
3. **Instructor quality** -- Real industry practitioners, not just teachers
4. **Portfolio/project output** -- Something tangible to show
5. **Price** -- Willing to invest $1,000-$2,500 for intensive programs

**Price sensitivity:** MODERATE. Will pay premium for credible programs but need to see clear career ROI. The framing "investment in their future" resonates. Not comparing to free options -- comparing to cost of college prep tutors ($50-$150/hr).

**Key quotes from research:**
- "By 2030, over 65% of the jobs your child may hold don't exist yet" (recurring marketing angle)
- "83% of HR leaders believe workforce demand is developing faster than workers' skills" (WEF 2025)
- Parents are choosing "reliable, hands-on, structured careers" -- skilled trades (29%), corporate (28%), government (23%)

**What they DON'T want:**
- Generic "learn Python" courses with no AI component
- Purely theoretical content with no practical application
- Programs that feel like school (worksheets, tests)

### SEGMENT B: "Give My Teen a Productive Hobby"

**Size estimate:** ~15-20% of the market

**Core Job:**
When **my teenager spends 7+ hours a day consuming content on screens and I'm worried about their mental health and productivity**, I want **them to use technology creatively -- building, not scrolling**, so that **their screen time becomes developmental rather than destructive.**

**Trigger events:**
- Seeing screen time reports (7.5 hours/day average for 15-18 year olds)
- Teen showing signs of social media addiction or depression
- Summer approaching with no structured activities planned
- Reading about phone/social media harms to teens
- "I'd rather they create than consume" moment

**Demographic profile:**
- Parents aged 35-50
- Broad income range ($50K-$150K)
- Often less technical themselves
- Motivated by "harm reduction" rather than career planning
- Both mothers (primary) and fathers

**Decision criteria (ranked):**
1. **Engagement** -- "Will my kid actually stick with this?" Most important factor
2. **Safety** -- Background-checked instructors, safe online environment
3. **Fun factor** -- Must compete with TikTok and YouTube for attention
4. **Visible progress** -- Games, apps, or projects the teen can show friends
5. **Price** -- More price-sensitive; $20-$50/month or $150-$500 for camps

**Price sensitivity:** HIGH. Comparing to other "productive hobbies" -- music lessons ($30-$60/week), sports leagues ($100-$300/season), art classes. Free or low-cost options (Scratch, Code.org, Khan Academy) often tried first.

**Key data points:**
- "When your teen uses a screen to write, design, code, edit videos, or create -- that's high-value screen time" (2025 guidance)
- Framework: "80% productive screen use, 20% recreational" during study hours
- "Prioritize creating over consuming" is the core philosophy parents adopt
- Average teen: 6-9 hours of daily screen time, mostly consumption

**What they DON'T want:**
- Expensive programs they can't evaluate quickly
- Programs that feel like more homework
- Overly technical language ("neural networks") that alienates the teen
- Long commitments before seeing results

### SEGMENT C: "College Admission Advantage"

**Size estimate:** ~15-20% of the market

**Core Job:**
When **my teen is 14-16 and building their college application portfolio**, I want **them to have impressive STEM extracurriculars and possibly a coding competition win or published project**, so that **they stand out in college admissions for top universities.**

**Trigger events:**
- College admissions season anxiety (beginning sophomore/junior year)
- Learning that STEM extracurriculars are Tier 1-2 activities for admissions
- College counselor suggesting "show passion for intended major"
- Seeing peers' children with coding portfolios
- AP Computer Science enrollment decisions

**Demographic profile:**
- Parents aged 40-55
- Upper-middle to high income ($120K+)
- Often college-educated at selective institutions themselves
- Geographically concentrated: Northeast, Bay Area, Seattle, Austin, major metros
- "Tiger parent" or "helicopter parent" tendencies -- college is the #1 priority

**Decision criteria (ranked):**
1. **Prestige/selectivity of program** -- Tier 1-2 on CollegeVine scale matters
2. **Portfolio deliverable** -- Published app, hackathon win, or research project
3. **Brand recognition** -- Stanford, MIT, or well-known program names on resume
4. **Instructor credentials** -- University affiliations, industry pedigree
5. **Price** -- Least price-sensitive segment; will pay $2,000-$7,000

**Price sensitivity:** LOW. Already spending on SAT prep ($1,000-$3,000), college counselors ($3,000-$10,000), and other extracurriculars. Coding camp at $1,500-$4,000 is just another line item. Free programs like PROMYS (6 weeks, ~$7,000 before aid) are acceptable.

**Key data points:**
- "Once you clear the academic threshold at selective colleges, extracurriculars can be just as important as your grades"
- "Participating in a CS summer program clearly signals interest in the field and willingness to pursue learning outside the classroom"
- "Tiers 1 and 2 are reserved for the most impressive and rarest activities -- most summer programs are Tiers 3 and 4"
- Competitive programs (free, selective application) are valued as Tier 1-2

**What they DON'T want:**
- Programs that look "generic" on a college app
- Online-only courses (in-person at prestigious campuses preferred)
- Certificates from unknown platforms
- Anything their college counselor hasn't heard of

### SEGMENT D: "My Teen Wants to Build Things" (Teen-Driven Demand)

**Size estimate:** ~15-20% of the market

**Core Job (Parent perspective):**
When **my teenager is already motivated and asking to learn coding/AI/game dev**, I want **to find the right program that matches their existing interest and pushes them further**, so that **I support their natural curiosity and they develop a real skill.**

**Core Job (Teen perspective):**
When **I see what people build with code and AI on YouTube/TikTok/GitHub**, I want **to learn how to build my own apps, games, or AI tools**, so that **I can create cool things, maybe make money, and feel in control of technology.**

**Trigger events:**
- Teen discovers Cursor/Replit/GitHub Copilot and starts building
- Teen sees a peer build an app or website
- Teen watches coding tutorials on YouTube
- Teen participates in a hackathon at school
- Teen starts a small business and needs a website/app
- "Mom/Dad, I want to learn Python" moment

**Demographic profile:**
- Teen: 13-17, predominantly male (but growing female segment via Girls Who Code), tech-curious, often already a "power user" of technology
- Parent: Varies widely; often less technical than the child
- Income: Broad range; the teen's motivation is the driver, not the parent's anxiety

**Decision criteria (teen-driven, parent-approved):**
1. **What can I BUILD** -- Games, apps, AI tools, websites; tangible output
2. **Peer/community** -- Other teens who code; Discord, hackathons, teams
3. **Speed to first result** -- "Hello World" to "real app" in days, not months
4. **Cool technology** -- AI, game engines, app development, not spreadsheets
5. **Price (parent)** -- Moderate sensitivity; will invest $500-$2,000 if teen shows commitment

**Price sensitivity:** MODERATE. Parent sees this as supporting a demonstrated interest (similar to buying a good guitar for a teen who already plays). Willing to invest more than Segment B because the teen has already shown motivation.

**Key data points:**
- 32% of surveyed students want to transition to entrepreneurship within 5 years of graduation
- 17% of surveyed undergrad students already own a business
- "Powerful new AI tools like GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Lovable, and Replit have given even people with little to no knowledge of coding the ability to build impressive-looking apps"
- "Venture capital firms and startup programs -- including Y Combinator -- no longer have strict age minimums"
- Teens in AI Global Techathon inspires teens aged 13-18 to solve real-world problems through AI

**What teens want to build (ranked by interest):**
1. **Games** -- 2D/3D, interactive, dynamic NPCs with AI
2. **Social/content apps** -- Think "next TikTok" or tools for content creators
3. **AI-powered tools** -- Chatbots, image generators, automation
4. **E-commerce** -- Shopify stores, dropshipping tools
5. **Problem-solving apps** -- Personal finance trackers, study tools, health apps
6. **Hackathon projects for social good** -- Climate, health, education

**What they DON'T want:**
- Boring, curriculum-based learning (feels like school)
- "Coding for beginners" when they're already intermediate
- No-code tools that don't teach real skills
- Programs where they can't choose their own project

### SEGMENT E: "I Don't Understand AI, But My Kid Should"

**Size estimate:** ~10-15% of the market

**Core Job:**
When **I hear about AI everywhere but I don't personally understand it and feel left behind**, I want **someone trustworthy to teach my child what I can't**, so that **my child doesn't suffer the same confusion and disadvantage I feel.**

**Trigger events:**
- Feeling lost when their child talks about ChatGPT
- Workplace introducing AI tools the parent struggles with
- News articles about AI that feel incomprehensible
- Other parents' children doing impressive AI projects
- School mentioning AI in curriculum without parent understanding

**Demographic profile:**
- Parents aged 40-55
- Often non-technical careers (teachers, nurses, small business, trades, service industry)
- Lower-to-middle income ($40K-$90K)
- May have high school diploma or associate's degree (only 31% of non-BA parents say schools should teach AI, vs 62% of BA+ parents)
- Often immigrant or first-generation families who see education as the path up

**Decision criteria (ranked):**
1. **Trust in the provider** -- Reviews, word-of-mouth, "safe" brand
2. **Simplicity of enrollment** -- Can't evaluate curriculum quality themselves
3. **Child's enthusiasm** -- "My kid likes it" is the primary success metric
4. **Price** -- Very price-sensitive; $0-$30/month or free programs preferred
5. **Language/accessibility** -- May need bilingual options

**Price sensitivity:** VERY HIGH. Comparing to "can my kid learn this for free?" First stop: Khan Academy, Code.org, YouTube tutorials. Will pay $7-$30/month for structured platforms (Tynker at $7.50/mo, CodaKid at $29/mo).

**Key data points:**
- 54% of parents feel "somewhat confident" in general AI understanding, but confidence drops for parenting decisions
- "Parents worry about a widening AI literacy gap between students and their teachers, with kids racing ahead"
- Income-based gap: in 2025, the gap between high-income and low-income teens' AI usage reached 24 percentage points (doubled from 12 points in 2024)
- 85% of parents are at least "somewhat interested" in learning more about AI to support their child

**What they DON'T want:**
- Being made to feel ignorant or "behind"
- Jargon-heavy marketing
- Programs that require parent technical involvement
- Expensive programs with unclear value

### SEGMENT F (DISCOVERED): "Responsible AI Citizen"

**Size estimate:** ~5-10% of the market

**Core Job:**
When **I see my teen using AI tools without understanding the ethics, privacy risks, or potential for manipulation**, I want **them to learn not just HOW to use AI but how to use it RESPONSIBLY**, so that **they become thoughtful, ethical technology users who can't be exploited.**

**Trigger events:**
- Learning their teen uses AI companions for emotional support
- News about AI-generated deepfakes affecting teens
- Discovering teen shared personal information with chatbots
- Stories about AI chatbot suicide links or exploitation
- School cheating incidents involving AI

**Demographic profile:**
- Parents aged 35-55
- Higher education levels
- Often educators, healthcare workers, or policy-involved professionals
- Middle-to-upper income
- More likely female

**Decision criteria (ranked):**
1. **Ethics/safety curriculum** -- Digital citizenship, AI literacy, critical thinking
2. **Provider values** -- Not just a tech company, but one that cares about kids
3. **Parent involvement** -- Transparency into what child is learning
4. **Practical skills** -- Wants ethics + technical, not just theory
5. **Price** -- Moderate sensitivity

**Key data points:**
- 84% of parents worry about AI misusing kids' data
- 3/4 of parents AND kids support government oversight of AI
- 8 in 10 parents want more guardrails on AI for their children
- 97% of parents surveyed expressed at least one worry about AI integration in schools

1. **Games** (highest interest)
   - 2D/3D game development
   - AI-powered NPCs with dynamic dialogue
   - Mobile games they can share with friends

2. **AI-Powered Tools**
   - Chatbots and AI assistants
   - Image generators and filters
   - Automation tools for personal use

3. **Social & Content Apps**
   - Apps for friend groups
   - Content creation tools
   - Augmented reality filters (via Vidcode etc.)

4. **E-Commerce & Money-Making**
   - Shopify stores and dropshipping
   - Personal brand websites
   - Side business tools

5. **Problem-Solving Apps**
   - Personal finance trackers
   - Study aids and homework helpers
   - Health and fitness trackers

6. **Social Good Projects**
   - Climate and environment solutions (hackathon favorites)
   - Educational tools for underserved communities
   - Healthcare accessibility apps

### 5.2 The "Vibe Coding" Revolution

A massive shift in 2025-2026: tools like Cursor, Replit, Lovable, and GitHub Copilot mean teens can build functional apps with minimal traditional coding knowledge. This changes the landscape:
- Lowers barrier to entry dramatically
- Shifts focus from syntax to problem-solving and design
- Creates a new category: "AI-augmented builders" vs. "traditional coders"
- MIT Technology Review (Dec 2025): "AI coding is now everywhere"

## Part 6: Decision Journey Map

### 6.1 Typical Decision Timeline

**Stage 1: Awareness (Weeks/Months before enrollment)**
- Parent reads/hears about AI disrupting jobs
- Conversation with teen about future
- Social media exposure to "teens building AI projects"

**Stage 2: Research (1-3 weeks)**
- Google searches: "best coding camp for teens 2026"
- Reddit/forum reading for reviews
- Asking other parents
- Checking CollegeVine/TeenLife for program lists

**Stage 3: Evaluation (3-7 days)**
- Comparing 2-4 programs
- Checking prices, schedules, locations
- Showing options to teen for buy-in
- Reading reviews/testimonials

**Stage 4: Decision (1-3 days)**
- Teen approval is often the tiebreaker
- Early bird deadlines create urgency
- Sibling discounts influence timing

**Stage 5: Post-purchase validation**
- "Did my kid enjoy it?"
- "Can they show me what they built?"
- "Would they want to do it again?"

### 6.2 Key Decision Influencers

1. **The teenager themselves** -- If teen says no, purchase doesn't happen
2. **Other parents** -- Word of mouth is the #1 trust channel
3. **School counselors** -- Especially for Segment C
4. **Online reviews** -- Reddit particularly influential
5. **Program website** -- Student projects/portfolio gallery is crucial
6. **Price anchoring** -- Free trial or money-back guarantee reduces risk

## Part 7: Key Insights for Product Development

### 7.1 Underserved Needs

1. **The "AI-first" curriculum gap** -- Most teen coding programs still teach traditional coding. Very few teach "how to build with AI" as the primary skill. This is the biggest market gap.

2. **The parent education gap** -- 85% of parents want to learn more about AI themselves. Programs that educate BOTH parent and teen would be highly differentiated.

3. **The portfolio/outcome gap** -- Parents (especially Segments A and C) want a clear deliverable. Programs that guarantee a published app, portfolio site, or project showcase have a massive advantage.

4. **The "productive screen time" framing** -- For Segment B, the pitch isn't "learn to code" -- it's "transform consuming into creating." This reframe resonates deeply.

5. **The speed-to-result gap** -- Teens want to build something functional in DAYS, not months. AI-assisted development tools make this possible. Programs still using 6-month curricula before students build anything real are losing to faster alternatives.

6. **The affordability gap** -- Segment E (the largest underserved segment by dollar volume) has almost no premium options designed for them. Most programs target affluent families.

### 7.2 Winning Positioning by Segment

| Segment | Winning Message |
|---------|----------------|
| A: Future-Proof | "Your teen won't compete against AI -- they'll command it" |
| B: Productive Hobby | "From scrolling to building: turn screen time into skill time" |
| C: College Advantage | "The portfolio that gets them into [dream school]" |
| D: Teen-Driven | "Build your first AI app this week" |
| E: Don't Understand AI | "We'll teach them what school can't" |
| F: Responsible AI | "Not just how to use AI -- how to use it wisely" |

### 7.3 Market Size Indicators

- Global online coding for kids market: projected to hit **$22.30 billion by 2033** (CAGR 18.2%)
- AI in education market: **$7.57 billion in 2025**, growing at 38.4% CAGR
- Global coding bootcamp market: growing at 10.5% CAGR (2023-2028)
- ~170 million new technology-related jobs expected this decade (WEF), offset by 92 million displaced roles
