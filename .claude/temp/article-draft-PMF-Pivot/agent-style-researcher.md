## STRUCTURAL PATTERNS

### Article Length
- **Range:** 1,300 -- 3,700 words
- **Sweet spot:** 2,000 -- 3,500 words
- **Median across top articles:** ~2,300 words
- **Recommendation for our article:** 2,500 -- 3,000 words (enough depth for PMF + pivot + AI angle, without losing attention)

### Headline Patterns
Top-performing headlines fall into three types:
1. **How-to + promise:** "How to Find Minimum Viable Product-Market Fit (PMF)", "How to Build Products People Love in 2024 and Beyond"
2. **Numbered steps + specific outcome:** "5 Steps To Achieving Product Market Fit And The 40% Rule"
3. **Provocative question:** "Is AI Making Product Managers Obsolete?"

Common features:
- Max 80 characters (HackerNoon guideline)
- Title case
- Specific, not vague -- names the exact concept (PMF, pivot, AI)
- Often includes a number OR a named framework/rule
- Zero clickbait -- the headline is a genuine promise of content

**Our headline "How to Find Product-Market Fit Fast -- and Pivot the Right Way" scores well:** it is how-to, specific, dual-promise, fits under 80 chars.

### Hook (First 2-3 Sentences)
Three dominant hook types across analysed articles:

**Type A -- Alarming statistic (most common):**
> "Over 90% of startups fail. So, out of 100 startups, only 10 will survive for longer than 5-10 years."

**Type B -- Personal story drop:**
> "In mid-May, we were just closing our seed round after an 8-week-long fundraising process. Two weeks later, we started to have concerns over our product and vision."

**Type C -- Problem statement + empathy:**
> "From what I have seen working with companies, there are 2 core reasons that a company isn't growing: They don't know what to do. They don't know how to do it effectively."

Pattern: The hook never starts with a definition. It starts with tension -- a stat that shocks, a personal moment of crisis, or a problem the reader already feels.

### Paragraph Length
- **Short paragraphs dominate:** 2-4 sentences is the standard
- **Maximum:** 5-6 sentences for complex explanations
- **Single-sentence paragraphs** are used for emphasis (1-2 per article)
- **No dense blocks** -- anything over 6 sentences gets broken up
- **Average:** 3 sentences per paragraph

### Subheadings
- **Frequency:** Every 200-400 words (one subheading per ~300 words)
- **Count per article:** 5-9 subheadings
- **Style:** Mix of questions ("What do I need to achieve a good PMF?"), numbered steps ("Step 1. A good business model"), and declarative statements ("Loss of faith is the trigger to pivot")
- **Recommendation for 2,800-word article:** 7-9 subheadings

### Bullet/Numbered Lists
- **Frequency:** 3-7 lists per article
- **Placement:** After explaining a concept, the list unpacks it into actionable components
- **Length:** 3-6 items per list (not more)
- **Format:** Both bulleted (unordered) and numbered (ordered) are used -- numbered for sequential steps, bulleted for parallel options
- **Key rule:** Lists break complexity into scannable chunks -- they never replace narrative entirely

### Conclusion Patterns
Three types observed:

1. **Iterative call-back** (best for how-to): "Return to steps 3, 4, and 5 again and again until the 40% rule is achieved."
2. **Forward-looking motivational:** "We have become passionate again, this time with our new vision."
3. **Action-oriented warning:** "Without understanding how your work is moving towards your goals, you'll just keep rowing forever."

Pattern: HackerNoon closings are SHORT (2-4 sentences). They never "summarize the article." They either push the reader to act, create urgency, or invite conversation ("What do you think?").

## TONAL PATTERNS

### Formality Scale: 4/10 (conversational-professional)
HackerNoon tone is distinctly informal compared to Harvard Business Review or McKinsey. It reads like a smart colleague explaining over coffee, not a professor lecturing.

### First Person ("I", "we")
- **Heavily used.** HackerNoon explicitly requires first person in their editorial guidelines: "Write in first person -- share your authentic story."
- "I" is used for personal experience, opinions, analysis
- "We" is used for shared industry experience ("we've all been there") -- but HackerNoon guidelines warn against overusing "we" (sounds corporate)
- **Recommendation:** Use "I" for personal insights and experience; "we" sparingly for shared professional community moments

### Second Person ("you")
- **Very common.** Used to make advice actionable and direct
- "You need to...", "Your team should...", "If you find yourself..."
- Creates immediacy and reader engagement
- **Recommendation:** Alternate between "I did X" (credibility) and "You should do Y" (actionability)

### Humor/Irony
- **Light touch.** Not absent, but subtle
- Examples: "Will AI Kill Product Management or Just Be a Really Fun Intern?", casual asides, emoji in some articles
- Never sarcastic or self-deprecating to a fault
- **Recommendation:** 1-2 light moments per article. Do NOT try to be funny throughout -- it undermines authority on HackerNoon

### Expert vs. Conversational Tone
- **Ratio: 60% conversational / 40% expert**
- Expert credibility comes from: personal experience, specific frameworks, data points, named companies
- Conversational accessibility comes from: short sentences, direct address, metaphors, casual transitions
- **Key insight:** The best HackerNoon articles sound like someone who HAS DONE the thing telling you how -- not someone who READ ABOUT the thing summarizing it

### Personal Experience
- **Essential.** 6 out of 8 analysed articles include direct personal experience
- "When we pivoted...", "From my time at Uber...", "I analyzed over three hours of podcasts..."
- HackerNoon editorial guidelines explicitly state: "real stories by real tech professionals"
- **Recommendation:** Author (Ivan Zamesin) should include 2-3 personal anecdotes from working with startups, product teams, or his own product experience

## CONTENT PATTERNS

### Data/Statistics
- **Frequency:** 3-6 data points per article in the best-performing pieces
- **Placement:** Hook (opening stat), middle (supporting argument), conclusion (validating framework)
- **Style:** Woven into narrative, not dumped in tables
- **Sources:** Named (Accenture, McKinsey, Forbes) -- adds credibility
- **Example patterns:**
  - "Over 90% of startups fail" (hook)
  - "55% of respondents believe their data will likely be leaked" (mid-article support)
  - "A good LTV:CAC ratio is 3:1 or higher" (framework anchor)
- **Recommendation:** Include 4-5 data points. At least 1 in the hook, 2-3 in the body, 1 near the conclusion.

### Expert Quotes
- **Frequency:** 1-3 per article
- **Format:** Short inline quotes, attributed with name + role. Not long block quotes.
- **Example:** Eric Feng quote: "Product management will always be necessary, but the definition of the role will change over time."
- **Recommendation:** Use 2-3 expert quotes. Keep them under 20 words. Attribute clearly: Name, Role.

### Company Case Studies
- **Frequency:** 2-4 company examples per article
- **Depth:** Brief -- 2-4 sentences each, not deep dives
- **Style:** Used to ILLUSTRATE a point, not to narrate the company's full history
- **Companies commonly cited:** Amazon, Airbnb, Slack, Netflix, Superhuman, Dropbox
- **Example pattern:** "Slack started as a gaming company. The team realized their internal messaging tool was more valuable than the game itself -- and pivoted." (2 sentences, makes the point, moves on)
- **Recommendation:** Include 3-4 brief company examples (Superhuman, Airbnb, Slack, and one lesser-known/fresh example)

### Actionable Advice
- **Format:** Step-by-step frameworks are the gold standard
- **Structure per section:**
  1. Problem statement (why this matters)
  2. Framework/principle (the mental model)
  3. Action items (what to do -- often as a list)
  4. Metric/signal (how to know it worked)
- **Recommendation:** Each major section should end with a concrete "do this" takeaway

### Frameworks/Formulas
- **Heavily used.** Named frameworks give articles shareability and memorability
- Examples from analysed articles: "The 40% Rule", "Bullseye Framework", "ICE Scoring", "Vitamin vs. Advil", "Explore-Exploit Loop", "Business Model Canvas"
- **Recommendation:** Name our frameworks explicitly. Examples from theses: "Learning Velocity", "Hypothesis Pipeline", "Traction Map", "RAT Framework". Give each a clear label.

## HACKERNOON EDITORIAL REQUIREMENTS (from official guidelines)

1. **Write in first person.** Not "we" (sounds corporate). Use "I" for personal voice.
2. **Headline: max 80 characters, title case, zero clickbait.** Must convey self-interest, news value, or curiosity.
3. **Opening paragraph must set up a burning question** that compels continued reading. Make clear what the reader will learn.
4. **Every sentence should entice the reader to read the next one.**
5. **Use paragraphs, subheadings, bulleted/numbered lists** for readability.
6. **Include at least one image** (original preferred). Use graphs and infographics.
7. **Tag with 5-8 relevant terms** (SEO-oriented).
8. **Share sources and attribute quotes.** Include 1-2 lines about your background.
9. **Avoid:** essay style, "Top/Best Of" lists, review articles, SEO spam, gambling/narcotics content.
10. **Key metric HackerNoon tracks: READ TIME** (how long users actively engage), not claps or likes.

## STYLE GUIDE for HackerNoon

**Headline:** How-to format with specific promise. Max 80 chars. Include the core concept name (PMF, pivot). Numbers or named rules add clickability. Title case. No clickbait.

**Hook:** Open with tension -- either a startling statistic ("X% of startups fail to find PMF"), a personal crisis moment ("We were 3 months in and our metrics were flatline"), or a bold contrarian claim. NEVER start with a definition. First 2-3 sentences must create a "why should I keep reading" pull.

**Paragraphs:** 2-4 sentences standard. Max 5-6 for complex explanation. Use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis (1-2 per article). No walls of text.

**Subheadings:** Every 250-350 words. 7-9 for a 2,800-word article. Mix types: questions ("When should you pivot?"), steps ("Step 2: Validate the job, not the solution"), and declarative claims ("Distribution is the real moat"). Each subheading should make the reader want to read that section even if they skipped everything above.

**Tone:** Conversational-professional. Formality 4/10. Write like a smart practitioner, not a professor. Use contractions ("don't", "isn't", "you'll"). Avoid jargon without context. Light humor OK (1-2 moments), but don't try to be a comedian.

**Voice:** First person ("I") for personal experience and opinions. Second person ("you") for advice and calls to action. Alternate between "I've seen this pattern" and "Here's what you should do." Avoid royal "we" -- sounds corporate. HackerNoon requires authentic personal voice.

**Bullet/numbered lists:** 3-5 lists per article. Use numbered lists for sequential steps, bulleted for parallel items. 3-6 items per list. Always precede a list with a sentence that sets it up. Never let the whole article be just lists.

**Expert quotes:** 2-3 per article. Keep under 20 words. Inline format: "As Marty Cagan puts it: '[quote]'." Always include Name + Role. No long block quotes.

**Data points:** 4-5 per article. At least 1 in the hook, 2-3 in the body, 1 near conclusion. Always name the source. Weave into narrative -- do not dump in isolation.

**Company examples:** 3-4 brief case studies (2-4 sentences each). Mix well-known (Airbnb, Slack) and fresh/lesser-known. Use to ILLUSTRATE a principle, not to tell a company biography.

**Named frameworks:** Give every key concept a memorable name: "Learning Velocity", "Hypothesis Pipeline", "The 40% Rule". Named frameworks get shared and remembered.

**Conclusion:** SHORT (2-4 sentences). Do NOT summarize the article. Either: (a) push to action ("Start building your hypothesis pipeline today"), (b) create urgency ("The window for slow iteration is closing"), or (c) invite dialogue ("What signals triggered YOUR last pivot?"). End with energy, not a recap.

**Images:** Include at least 1 image (HackerNoon requirement). Ideally: 1 original diagram/framework visualization + 1 supporting image. Graphs and infographics add credibility and break text.

**PROHIBITED:**
- Starting with a definition ("Product-market fit is defined as...")
- Essay/academic style (long paragraphs, passive voice, no personal experience)
- "Top 10 list" format -- HackerNoon explicitly rejects these
- Dense jargon without explanation
- Corporate "we" voice
- Paragraphs longer than 6 sentences
- Conclusions that summarize everything above
- Clickbait headlines that don't deliver
- Generic advice without named frameworks, data, or examples
- Writing without personal experience -- HackerNoon wants "real stories by real tech professionals"

## TOP-3 TECHNIQUES from Best Articles

### 1. "Vitamin vs. Advil" Metaphor Anchoring

The most shared HackerNoon articles anchor a complex concept to a vivid, sticky metaphor that the reader carries through the entire piece.

**How it works:** In "Knowing When to Hard Pivot," the author frames the entire pivot decision through one metaphor: "Is your product a vitamin or an Advil?" A vitamin is nice-to-have. An Advil solves acute pain. The article never lets go of this metaphor -- every argument is filtered through it. Readers remember "Vitamin vs. Advil" long after forgetting the article details.

**Application to our article:** Create 1-2 anchor metaphors for the core concepts. Example: "Finding PMF is not a treasure hunt -- it's a metal detector sweep. You don't wander hoping to stumble on gold. You systematically scan, get a signal, dig, test, and move to the next grid square." Or for the pivot: "A pivot is not an escape hatch -- it's a course correction. The ship stays the same. The destination stays the same. Only the route changes."

---

### 2. "Personal Failure Drop" Hook

The highest-engagement openings on HackerNoon begin with a moment of personal vulnerability or failure, THEN pivot to the framework that fixed it.

**How it works:** In "Pre-PMF Startup: How To Do It Right," the author opens: "In the beginning of June 2020, we had enough cash to sustain ourselves for 2 years, and had decided to pivot." This is not a statistic. It is a human moment -- the reader instantly empathizes with the uncertainty of a team sitting on runway and starting over. THEN the author delivers the framework for how they navigated it.

**Application to our article:** Open with a real moment from Ivan Zamesin's experience: a specific team, a specific moment of "we're building the wrong thing," a specific pivot decision. Then transition: "That experience led me to develop a framework I now teach to 12,000+ product managers." The personal story earns the right to prescribe.

---

### 3. "Numbered Framework + Specific Metric" Structure

The most actionable (and most bookmarked) HackerNoon articles give the reader a numbered sequence of steps, EACH anchored to a measurable signal.

**How it works:** In "5 Steps To Achieving Product Market Fit And The 40% Rule," every step has: (a) what to do, (b) how to know you did it right. Step 2 (Market Validation) = "Create a landing page + run paid ads. Track 5% conversion rate. Below target? Return to Step 1." This is not vague advice -- it is a recipe with measurements.

**Application to our article:** Structure the core of the article as a numbered pipeline (e.g., "The 5-Step Hypothesis Pipeline") where EACH step has:
- A clear action ("Run 10 problem interviews in 7 days")
- A measurable signal ("If 6+ describe the same pain unprompted, proceed")
- A pivot trigger ("If fewer than 3, return to hypothesis generation")

This format is what makes readers screenshot articles and share them in Slack channels.
