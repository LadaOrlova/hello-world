### Hook H2: Jasper AI -- from $120M ARR to $35M in 12 months when ChatGPT ate its lunch

In January 2023, Jasper AI was projecting $250M ARR by end of 2024. They had raised $125M at a $1.5B valuation. They were THE AI writing tool. Then ChatGPT went viral. Traffic fell 30% in two months. Revenue crashed from $120M to $35M -- a 53% decline. Both co-founders were out by September 2023. Three pivots in 12 months: GPT-3 wrapper, OpenAI partnership attempt, proprietary model attempt. Employee equity marked down 20%. The company that had found perfect PMF in AI writing had its fit vaporized overnight -- not because the product got worse, but because the market shifted beneath them.

**Why it works as a hook:** Dramatic reversal of fortune. Concrete numbers ($120M to $35M). Shows PMF is not a destination. Fresh (2023-2025). Cautionary tale that sets up the treadmill thesis.

### Thesis A3: "Your idea is already dead" -- find the cause of death in assumptions

#### Case 3.1: Quibi -- $1.8B burned on unvalidated assumptions

**Company:** Quibi
**Person:** Jeffrey Katzenberg, Meg Whitman
**What happened:** Quibi raised $1.8B to build premium short-form mobile video. The killer assumption: people want high-production-value content in 10-minute chunks on their phones, specifically during commutes and waiting times. This was never validated. No MVP, no experimental beta, no user testing. They assumed mobile viewing was primary; studies showed millennials consumed most video on TVs. They assumed short-form premium was unserved; TikTok and YouTube already owned the space. They assumed commute-time viewing was a massive TAM; the pandemic eliminated commutes. Shut down 6 months after launch. $1.8B proof that assumptions, not code, are the killer.
**Numbers:** $1.8B raised; shut down in 6 months; zero PMF validation before launch; 0 MVP testing.

#### Case 3.2: Notion -- nearly died in 2015, reborn by finding the real cause of death

**Company:** Notion
**Person:** Ivan Zhao, co-founder/CEO
**What happened:** In 2015, Notion was running out of money. Their first product was a programming application "easy to use without coding knowledge" -- but nobody wanted it. The assumption that non-coders wanted a coding-like tool was the cause of death. Zhao fired all 4 employees, moved from San Francisco to Kyoto, Japan, and rebuilt from scratch. The critical insight: "most people don't wake up wanting to build software; they wake up to get stuff done." They pivoted to a modular workspace (notes + wikis + databases). Notion 2.0 launched in March 2018 and captured 1 million users within months. Valued at $10B. The cause of death of Notion 1.0 was a wrong assumption about what job users were hiring the product for.

### Thesis A4: PM/founder as hypothesis-factory operator

#### Case 4.1: Spotify -- 520 experiments, 12% win rate, 64% learning rate

**Company:** Spotify
**What happened:** Spotify runs one of the most sophisticated hypothesis-testing operations in tech. In their "Experiments with Learning" (EwL) framework published in September 2025, they revealed: 520 experiments across 58 teams in a year, with a win rate of only ~12% but a learning rate of ~64%. The key insight: most value comes NOT from shipping winners, but from discovering what NOT to ship. Preventing regressions is as important as chasing wins. They shifted the success metric from "experiment velocity" (how many experiments per week) to "learning velocity" (how many validated learnings per week). This is the hypothesis-factory operator mindset in action at scale.
**Numbers:** 520 experiments/year; 58 teams; ~12% win rate; ~64% learning rate; key metric = learning velocity, not experiment velocity.

### Thesis A5: Killer assumption (probability x consequences / test cost)

#### Case 5.1: Quibi -- $1.8B bet on an untested killer assumption

(See Case 3.1 above -- Quibi is the definitive killer assumption case)

The killer assumption formula applied to Quibi:
- Probability the assumption was wrong: HIGH (no prior evidence of demand for premium short-form mobile video)
- Consequences of being wrong: CATASTROPHIC ($1.8B and the entire company)
- Cost to test it: LOW (could have done a landing page test, a beta with sample content, or even a quick survey for <$50K)
- **Risk Score = extremely high, but they never ran the test**

**Thesis:** A5

#### Case 5.2: Dropbox -- $0 validation of the killer assumption via a 3-minute video

**Company:** Dropbox
**Person:** Drew Houston, founder
**What happened:** Dropbox's killer assumption was: "People experience a file-sync problem painful enough to adopt a new tool." Instead of building the product, Houston created a 3-minute demo video and shared it on Hacker News in April 2007. Overnight, the waitlist exploded from 5,000 to 75,000 signups. Cost of the test: one video, one evening. This validated the desirability hypothesis before writing a single line of product code. The killer assumption formula applied: probability of being wrong = medium, consequences = total (if nobody cares, nothing to build), cost to test = near-zero (video). Houston tested the highest-risk, highest-consequence assumption at the lowest possible cost.
**Numbers:** 5K to 75K waitlist overnight; 3-minute video; $0 test cost; validated before product existed.
**URL:** https://thriveandgrow.com/2025/05/17/pre-launch-traction-how-dropbox-got-75000-signups/
**URL:** https://www.shortform.com/blog/dropbox-mvp-explainer-video/
**Year:** 2007 (classic, but definitive example of the formula)
**Thesis:** A5

#### Case 5.3: Popsa -- x4 installs from testing a single word assumption

**Company:** Popsa (photo book app)
**Person:** Matt Lerner, growth advisor (ex-PayPal)

The cost to test: one round of user interviews. The consequences of not testing: leaving 75% of potential installs on the table.

### Thesis N2: "Glass Slipper" cohort -- PMF is cohort-specific, not average

#### Case 7.1: a16z "Glass Slipper" Effect -- PMF is about THE RIGHT cohort, not the average

**Company:** a16z research (across AI industry)
**What happened:** Andreessen Horowitz coined the "Cinderella Glass Slipper Effect" in 2025 based on analysis of 100+ trillion tokens of AI model usage via OpenRouter. The pattern: users try solution after solution, model after model, like Cinderella's stepsister trying on shoes. When a specific cohort discovers a "workload-model fit," they lock in permanently. Gemini 2.5 Pro's June 2025 launch cohort retained ~35% of users at 5 months. Claude 4 Sonnet's May 2025 cohort retained ~40% at 4 months. BUT later cohorts (Sept-Oct 2025) that tried the same models churned drastically. The first cohort "claimed" the best use cases; later users were "explorers" who didn't find Cinderella-grade fit. PMF is not the average across all users -- it is the specific cohort that found the glass slipper.
**Numbers:** Gemini 2.5 Pro June cohort: ~35% retention at 5 months; Claude 4 Sonnet May cohort: ~40% at 4 months; later cohorts churned heavily; data from 100T+ tokens.
**URL:** https://a16z.com/the-cinderella-glass-slipper-effect-retention-rules-in-the-ai-era/
**URL:** https://a16z.com/state-of-ai/
**Year:** 2025
**Thesis:** N2

#### Case 7.2: Superhuman -- PMF score doubled by focusing on the "very disappointed" cohort

**Company:** Superhuman (AI email client)
**Person:** Rahul Vohra, founder/CEO
**What happened:** When Superhuman started using the Sean Ellis PMF survey in 2017, only 22% of users said they'd be "very disappointed" without the product (40% = PMF threshold). Instead of trying to please everyone, Vohra segmented users and identified the specific cohort that loved Superhuman: productivity-obsessed professionals who valued speed above all. He built a roadmap with 50% of resources on what this cohort already loved (doubling down on differentiators) and 50% on addressing objections from "on-the-fence" users who looked like the core cohort. In 3 quarters, the PMF score doubled from 22% to 58%. The product eventually reached $36M ARR and was acquired by Grammarly in July 2025. The lesson: PMF is not about your average user. It's about finding the cohort that would be "very disappointed" without you, and serving them fanatically.
**Numbers:** PMF score: 22% to 58% in 3 quarters; 40% threshold; $36M ARR; acquired by Grammarly (July 2025); 50/50 resource allocation.
**URL:** https://review.firstround.com/how-superhuman-built-an-engine-to-find-product-market-fit/
**URL:** https://digidai.github.io/2025/11/23/rahul-vohra-superhuman-grammarly-acquisition-pmf-framework-deep-analysis/
**URL:** https://techcrunch.com/2025/07/01/grammarly-acquires-ai-email-client-superhuman/
**Year:** 2017-2025
**Thesis:** N2

#### Case 7.3: Character AI -- novelty cohort vs. true-fit cohort, 28M to 20M MAU

**Company:** Character AI
**What happened:** Character AI exploded to 28M MAU in mid-2024 on the back of novelty -- everyone wanted to try AI chatbots. But by 2025, MAU dropped to ~20M as the "AI tourist" cohort churned out. a16z's research confirmed the pattern: AI products need to measure retention from Month 3 (M3), not M0, because the first months are inflated by curiosity-driven users who never had real fit. The "novelty cohort" (tourists) and the "true-fit cohort" (users who found a genuine job for the product) have completely different retention curves. Measuring average retention across both groups gives a misleading picture. Only the M3+ cohort reveals whether you have real PMF or just hype.
**Numbers:** 28M MAU peak to ~20M (2024-2025); 40M+ downloads but low conversion to durable engagement; M3 rebasing recommended by a16z.

#### Case 7.4: Airbnb -- "100 people who love you" = the original glass slipper

**Company:** Airbnb
**Person:** Brian Chesky, co-founder; Paul Graham, YC
**What happened:** Paul Graham told the struggling Airbnb founders: "It's better to have 100 customers that love you than a million customers that just sort of like you." Chesky took it literally. Instead of chasing growth, the founding team flew from Mountain View to New York to meet hosts personally, stayed in their homes, photographed listings. They found their "glass slipper" cohort: New York City hosts who needed income and had spare rooms. They served this tiny cohort obsessively until those 100 people loved them. Those 100 told 100, who told 100 more. Today Airbnb is worth ~$80B. The lesson: don't optimize for average satisfaction across a large base. Find 100 people who would be devastated without you.

## Bonus: Cross-cutting case

#### Case X1: Vapi -- from meeting notetaker to $130M voice AI platform by pivoting the segment

**Company:** Vapi (formerly Superpowered)
**What happened:** Vapi started as Superpowered, a Y Combinator-backed AI-powered meeting notetaker (crowded market, dozens of competitors). Now 250K+ developers and Fortune 500 customers. The segment pivot (from end-users of meeting tools to developers building voice agents) and the job pivot (from "take notes in meetings" to "build voice AI agents in minutes, not months") unlocked a completely different, much larger opportunity.
