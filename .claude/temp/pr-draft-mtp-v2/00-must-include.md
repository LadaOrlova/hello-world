## Thesis 1: The builder era is here

### BODY (section skeleton):
Boris Cherny, Head of Claude Code at Anthropic, hasn't edited a single line by hand since November 2025 — 95% of his code is AI-written, with +200% productivity per engineer. A quarter of Y Combinator's W25 batch ships with 95% AI-generated codebases. Collins Dictionary named "vibe coding" Word of the Year 2025. Marc Andreessen calls it a "Mexican standoff" between PM, engineering, and design — each believes they can do the other two roles with AI, and "they're actually all kind of correct." LinkedIn formalized this in December 2025, scrapping its APM program and launching "Associate Product Builder" — new hires learn coding, design, and PM together in cross-trained pods. The reframe that matters: a full-stack builder isn't a PM who just learned to code. It's a PM who can test dozens of hypotheses about the strongest constraints across segments, value propositions, activation, and business models — and maintain production quality through tests, reviews, economics. The old jobs — writing PRDs, creating mockups, moving Jira tasks — is disappearing in front of our eyes. You level up from pushing tasks to owning the entire value chain.

## Thesis 2: Speed without judgment is the feature mill on steroids

### BODY (section skeleton):
When AI crushes execution cost to near zero, the bottleneck moves upstream — to business judgment. It means diagnosing where in the causal chain the growth sits: Market segment, Jobs to be done, Strategy, Value proposition, Unit Economics, Acquisition Channels. You're not just optimizing features. You're bringing growth to business. Cagan: AI makes "PM theater" — PRDs, roadmaps, user stories — cheap and replaceable by agents. The PM who moves tickets is exactly who gets replaced. Mehta: shift from craft to judgment — when anyone can produce something polished, knowing what to create and why is the scarce asset. Without judgment, vibe coding is the feature mill on steroids. 90% of resources already go to features that never find a market. AI makes that 10x faster.

## Thesis 3: From PRDs to hypothesis pipelines

### BODY (section skeleton):
When AI collapses the cost of building and routine operations, you can test 10–100x more hypotheses per sprint. The paradigm flips: you're not launching products — you're buying knowledge. Every prototype is a bet, and the bottleneck shifts from execution to the pipeline itself. The PM becomes an operator of a hypothesis factory — designing it, feeding it, and optimizing its throughput: business goal, causal graph of growth mechanics, research, riskiest assumption test, build, measure. Each step can be delegated to an AI agent — and increasingly is. Boris Cherny describes the frontier: "Claude is starting to come up with ideas. Looking through feedback, bug reports, telemetry." The agent doesn't just execute — it proposes. Shreyas Doshi: 3-4 person startups now match the impact of 30-50 person teams. But this advantage only works if delegation follows risk. AI agents are less competent employees — hand them low-risk tasks (A/B tests, data analysis, draft copy) and keep the high-risk decisions for yourself.

## Thesis 4: The full cycle isn't here yet

### BODY (section skeleton):
You can prototype in an afternoon. You cannot ship to production in an afternoon. The critical chain breaks at security, DevOps, and compliance — the slowest link determines your actual speed. AI makes creation cheap, but each component you add multiplies the probability of failure. The full cycle is 12-24 months away. Until then: kill criteria, reversibility, and circuit breakers.

## Thesis 5: The split is already happening

### BODY (section skeleton):
The PM market is fracturing K-shaped. WEF: 94% of leaders face critical skill shortages in AI-ready roles, yet 92% report up to 20% workforce overcapacity — expected to hit 30% by 2028. The forces are structural. Builder PMs feel strong pull forward — the value is enormous, the pain of the old model obvious, barriers are low. Traditional PMs feel equally strong pushback — habit is deeply embedded, fear of coding real. People overestimate their readiness to change by a factor of three. This is the innovator's dilemma in real time. Companies get disrupted not because they work badly, but because they work well enough to resist change. The PM who operates at local optimizations only gets replaced. The PM who works at the global level — which market, which segment, when to pivot — becomes irreplaceable. The reframe is not "adapt or die." It is: build the environment — training, access, psychological safety — that lets people adapt. Bifurcation is not about talent. It's about architecture.

## Close: Where to start

### BODY:
1. Build one prototype this week — not to ship, but to learn what your judgment tells you when testing costs nothing.
2. Find growth points in your business model — in activation to value, in positioning, in which segment you compete, which jobs you compete for, whether you can apply a strategic move, and in unit economics.
3. Find risks in backlog item: "If this assumption is wrong, what do we lose?" Rank by consequence, not by effort.
4. Talk to one customer this week. No AI can replicate what you learn from watching someone struggle with your product in real time.
5. Final line: The PM role isn't disappearing. But the PM who only coordinates is. The builder who judges — who knows what to build, for whom, and when to stop — that PM has never been more essential.
