## TASK 2: Extracted stylistic patterns

### A. Structural patterns

**Article length:**
- Range: 864--3,500 words
- Sweet spot for guest posts: 1,000--1,500 words (per editorial guidelines)
- Most-read AI articles cluster at 1,200--1,800 words
- Only one outlier exceeds 2,500 words (strategy guide -- justified by 4-part structure)
- **Recommendation for our article: 1,200--1,500 words (aligns with both media brief and MtP sweet spot)**

**Headline types:**
- Dominant pattern: **"How to [verb] [noun]"** -- action-oriented
  - "How to actually prove AI agent ROI"
  - "How to navigate product stagnation"
- Second pattern: **Contrarian/tension statement**
  - "What AI can't--and shouldn't--do for product managers"
  - "Disrupt or be disrupted"
- Third pattern: **Role + colon + promise**
  - "Precision over hype: A product manager's playbook for launching AI that lands"
  - "Product managers' role in making AI/ML systems more relevant"
- All headlines use **sentence case** (MtP house style)

**Paragraph length:**
- Short: 2--3 sentences (dominant in the best articles)
- Medium: 4--5 sentences (used for deeper explanations)
- Long: 6+ sentences (rare, only in weaker articles)
- **Best articles mix short and medium paragraphs, never exceeding 5 sentences**

**Subheading frequency:**
- Average: 1 subheading per 200--300 words
- Range: 4--8 subheadings in a 1,200--1,500 word article
- Format: Sentence case, descriptive, occasionally provocative
- Best practice: Subheadings that work as a standalone "skim path" through the article

**Use of bullet points/lists:**
- Present in 6 of 8 articles analyzed
- Typically 3--5 items per list
- Used for: key takeaways, warning signs, step sequences, quick frameworks
- Numbered lists for sequential processes; bullets for parallel items
- MtP editorial guide: Full stop after sentence bullets; no punctuation for fragment bullets

**Hook format (opening paragraph):**
- Maximum 55 words (MtP editorial requirement)
- Dominant pattern: **Tension/conflict setup** -- present a problem the reader recognizes
  - "If you work in product leadership right now, you are probably living in two realities." (Chigrinets)
  - "As a product manager, do you find the mere mention of the word 'AI' exhausting?" (Haynie)
  - "Many of the hardest problems aren't about building better models." (Sarkar)
- Second pattern: **Counterintuitive thesis**
  - "Sometimes, the best way to grow your product is actually to replace it." (Musat)
  - "Protecting human connections from the AI-ification of everything." (Wanger)
- **Never** starts with a dictionary definition, statistics dump, or "In today's world..." generic opener.

**Conclusion format:**
- Pattern 1: **Reframe + call to action** -- strongest pattern
  - "Stop trying to predict the future. Start funding the discovery period..." (Murkin)
  - "Precision--not speed--is what makes your product unforgettable." (Tripathi)
- Pattern 2: **Stakes-raising philosophical question**
  - "If product management is dead... much more than the title 'product manager' will have died." (Wanger)
- Pattern 3: **Practical takeaway list** -- 3--5 bullets summarizing key actions
- **Never** ends with "In conclusion..." or generic summary paragraph

### B. Tonal patterns

**Formality scale: 4/10** (where 1 = casual blog, 10 = academic paper)
- Conversational but professional
- Contractions used freely ("don't," "can't," "it's," "you're") -- MtP editorial guide explicitly encourages contractions
- Em dashes used extensively for informal emphasis and asides
- Short, punchy sentences mixed with longer analytical ones

**First person usage:**
- Present in 7 of 8 articles
- Pattern: "I've seen," "I learned," "In my experience," "When I was at [company]..."
- Used to establish credibility and share honest experience
- Not used throughout -- appears in hooks and key insight moments, then shifts to "you" address
- **Best articles use 1st person for 20--30% of paragraphs, "you" address for 50%, third person for 20%**

**Direct address ("you"):**
- Heavy usage across all top articles
- "If you're a PM facing this..." / "Here's what you can do..." / "You've probably noticed..."
- Creates peer-to-peer feeling rather than lecturer-to-student

**Expert vs. conversational tone:**
- Best MtP articles read like "a smart colleague explaining over coffee"
- Authors position themselves as experienced practitioners, not academics or consultants
- Credentials mentioned briefly (company name, role) but not dwelled upon
- Anti-pattern: no one uses "dear reader" or "one might argue" -- that is too formal

**Personal experience usage:**
- Real company examples from author's own career (Walmart, AWS, Elsewhen, etc.)
- Mistakes and failures shared openly -- "I quickly changed my mind," "Even I got caught up"
- Framed as lessons, not humble-brags

### C. Content patterns

**Data density:**
- Low to moderate: 1--3 data points per article
- Data used as "spice" not "main course"
- Best examples:
  - "77% of workers feel confused about how to use AI" (Haynie -- citing Slingshot report)
  - "80% of teams still don't involve engineering during ideation" (Atlassian State of Product)
  - "50% of product features do absolutely nothing" (Standish Group via Musat)
  - "20% of features drive 80% of engagement" (Musat)
- Data always embedded in argument flow, never in separate "data" sections
- Source cited inline, briefly: "(2024 Digital Work Trends Report, Slingshot)"

**Expert quote format:**
- Surprisingly rare -- only 2 of 8 articles use external expert quotes
- When used: brief, attributed inline, not block-quoted
- More common: author references expert's idea and paraphrases it
  - "Teresa Torres brought me back down to earth" (Wanger)
- **Pattern: Reference expert thinking, don't block-quote it**

**Case study format:**
- Mini case studies (1--3 paragraphs), not full Harvard-style cases
- Used as illustration of a principle, not as the main argument
- Examples: IBM Watson Health failure, Latitude/AI Dungeon cost explosion, Slack's pivot, Flatiron Health
- Often structured as: "[Company] tried [approach]. [Result]. [Lesson for PM]."
- **Pattern: Cautionary tales are more popular than success stories**

**Actionable advice format:**
- Numbered steps: "Step 1: Develop a strong voice" (Haynie)
- Named frameworks: "The 3x Rule," "The Latitude Test" (Chigrinets)
- Questions to ask yourself: "Does heavy usage improve or erode user margins?"
- "You can do this next sprint" framing -- concrete, not abstract

**Frameworks usage:**
- Original, named frameworks are a hallmark of the best articles
- 6 of 8 articles introduce at least one original or adapted framework
- Frameworks are simple: 3--5 components maximum
- Often accompanied by a visual/table
- Named memorably: "The Latitude Test," "The 3x Rule," "Five Layered Insights"

### D. Viral/citable traits

**What conflict opens the best pieces?**
1. **Hype vs. reality** -- "AI is everywhere but most teams can't make it work"
2. **Pressure vs. judgment** -- "Your CEO wants AI features but you know they'll fail"
3. **Automation vs. humanity** -- "AI can do the tasks but should it?"
4. **Speed vs. precision** -- "Everyone ships fast but nobody ships right"
- **Pattern: The best MtP openers pit an external pressure against the PM's professional judgment**

**What headline format works best?**
1. "How to [action] [object]" -- direct utility promise
2. "[Provocative claim]: [practical subtitle]" -- tension + promise
3. "What [topic] [can't/shouldn't/won't] [do]" -- contrarian framing
- Headlines under 12 words perform best
- Including "product manager" or "PM" in headline increases relevance signal

**What increases quotability?**
- **Named rules/tests:** "The 3x Rule," "The Latitude Test" -- memorable, shareable
- **Punchy one-liners:** "Precision--not speed--is what makes your product unforgettable"
- **Contrarian statements:** "Sometimes the best way to grow your product is to replace it"
- **Specific numbers + surprising facts:** "50% of features do absolutely nothing"
- **Pattern: Quotable = short sentence + unexpected juxtaposition**

## TASK 3: Supplementary style guide

### SUPPLEMENTARY STYLE GUIDE for Mind the Product
*(These rules apply WHERE the media brief is silent.)*

**Headlines:** Sentence case. Under 12 words. Format: "How to [verb] [object]" or "[Tension]: [promise]." Include "product manager" or "PM" for audience signal. Avoid clickbait -- MtP readers are senior PMs who will bounce on empty promises.

**Hook:** Maximum 55 words (MtP editorial requirement). Open with a tension the reader lives daily -- hype vs. reality, pressure vs. judgment. Use direct address ("you") or a counterintuitive claim. Never open with "In today's rapidly evolving..." or dictionary definitions.

**Paragraphs:** 2--4 sentences is the sweet spot. Never exceed 5 sentences. Mix short punchy paragraphs (for impact) with medium ones (for explanation). One idea per paragraph.

**Subheadings:** Every 200--300 words. Sentence case. Descriptive enough to create a "skim path" -- a reader should understand the article's argument from subheadings alone. Occasionally use a question or provocative statement as a subheading.

**Tone:** Conversational professional (4/10 formality). Use contractions freely. Write as a peer, not a professor. Em dashes for asides and emphasis. Short sentences for key points; longer ones for nuance. The voice is "experienced PM explaining to a smart colleague."

**Lists:** 3--5 items. Numbered for sequences, bullets for parallel items. Full stops after sentence-format bullets; no punctuation after fragment bullets (MtP house style). Use lists to break up dense sections, not as the primary content vehicle.

**Expert quotes:** Paraphrase and attribute rather than block-quoting. "As [Name] argues, [paraphrased idea]" or "[Name] showed that [insight]." Keep references brief -- MtP readers trust practitioner voice over appeal to authority.

**Data points:** 1--3 per article maximum. Embed in argument flow, never in separate data sections. Cite source briefly inline. Use surprising or counterintuitive stats for maximum impact. Avoid data walls.

**Case studies:** Mini-format: 1--3 paragraphs. Structure: "[Company] tried [approach] --> [Result] --> [Lesson for the reader]." Cautionary tales (failures, unexpected costs, pivots) resonate more than success stories. Always extract the transferable principle.

**Frameworks:** Introduce at least one original or adapted framework. Name it memorably (3--5 words). Keep it simple: 3--5 components. If possible, include a visual or table. The framework should be immediately applicable -- "try this in your next sprint."

**Conclusion:** Never "In conclusion..." Reframe the central tension with a decisive stance + one-line call to action. Or: 3--5 bullet summary of what to do Monday morning. End on a line worth quoting.

**Spelling & punctuation:** UK or US English -- consistent throughout (our article: US English). Oxford comma always. Ampersands never in body text. Numbers below 10 spelled out; 10+ as numerals. Em dashes with no spaces.

**Images:** 1600x900px header image. Original infographics of frameworks preferred over stock photos. Captions required.

**AVOID:**
- Academic or marketing tone ("leverage," "synergize," "paradigm shift")
- Generic AI hype without practical grounding
- Opening with "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
- Paragraphs longer than 5 sentences
- Block quotes from famous people without your own interpretation
- Content that reads as product/company advertising
- AP-style title case headings
- Ampersands in body text
- More than 3 data points -- this is not a research paper
- Abstract advice without "what to do next sprint" framing

### TOP-3 TECHNIQUES from the best articles

**1. The "Dual Reality" hook (from Chigrinets, Haynie, Wanger)**
Open by naming TWO opposing forces the PM lives between -- executive AI pressure vs. operational reality; automation promise vs. relationship erosion; hype cycle vs. budget constraints. This instantly signals "I understand your life" and creates tension that pulls the reader forward. Every top-performing MtP article creates conflict in the first paragraph.

**2. The "Named Framework" anchor (from Chigrinets, Tripathi, Murkin, Sarkar)**
Create one memorable, named concept (The 3x Rule, The Latitude Test, Six Habits for AI Launches, Four-Phase ROI Model) that the reader can carry away, reference in meetings, and share with colleagues. The framework should be simple enough to explain in one sentence and practical enough to use in the next planning cycle. This is the single strongest driver of social sharing on MtP.

**3. The "Cautionary Mini-Case" proof (from Haynie, Chigrinets, Wanger, Musat)**
Instead of celebrating successes, cite a well-known failure or unexpected cost. IBM Watson Health's collapse, Latitude's runaway AI costs, Spirit Airlines' data problems. These stories take 2--3 sentences and accomplish two things: they prove you're honest (not selling hype), and they make the reader feel smarter for not having made the same mistake. MtP readers trust warnings more than promises.

---

### VIRAL TRIGGERS to replicate

1. **"Even I got caught up in the hype"** -- Honest admission of your own mistakes makes you trustworthy. MtP's most-shared articles include at least one moment of author vulnerability.

2. **"X% of [thing you assumed] is actually [surprising truth]"** -- One well-placed, counterintuitive statistic anchors credibility and gives readers ammunition for internal conversations. The stat should challenge conventional wisdom.

3. **"Here's what to do Monday morning"** -- End sections with concrete next steps. MtP readers are practitioners who measure value by applicability. The most bookmarked articles include at least 3 "do this" moments.

4. **"The [Name] Test/Rule"** -- A branded decision-making heuristic that readers can take into their next meeting. This is the single most quotable and shareable element in MtP articles. If your article produces one named concept that enters the PM vocabulary, it has succeeded.

5. **"Stop [common practice]. Start [better alternative]."** -- Binary reframes are the most shared sentence format on MtP. "Stop trying to predict the future. Start funding discovery." "Stop chasing AI features. Start measuring what matters." Short, punchy, decisive.
