# Brief — How to Find Product-Market Fit Fast — and Pivot the Right Way

## Outlet: HackerNoon
## Word count: 1000-2000 (target ~1,800)

## Core idea
AI drastically cheapened and accelerated prototyping, but PMF is still found not in code but in evidence: segment -> job -> value -> sales/retention. The winner is not who "builds fastest" but who buys knowledge fastest and pivots correctly based on signals, not noise.

## Theses (verbatim from input file)

### Thesis A1: AI/vibe-coding flips the PMF cycle (but the bottleneck = validation)
AI lowers the prototyping barrier, so you can show people more "real" solutions more often and get objections/fears/value-fit signals faster. But code generation speed != knowledge acquisition speed: vibe-coding can be stochastic, and AI tools push toward "more code, less validation." The right algorithm: use AI as an artifact accelerator, but measure progress by the number of valid checks and signal quality.

### Thesis A2: You can quickly build value variants for segments/Jobs (but winning requires a wedge)
AI lets you quickly assemble and modify solution variants for different segments and Jobs — useful for divergence. But in consumer AI there's attention/subscription concentration: users often pick one main tool. The right process: test variants, but find the "foundational cohort" as early as possible and lock in 1 key workload/segment.

### Thesis A3: "The idea is already dead": the goal is to find the cause of death in assumptions, not "launch a product"
A new idea almost always rests on false assumptions. The right goal of the early stage is not "launch a product" but to buy knowledge about what exactly will kill it (market/segment/job/value/channel/economics), and to pivot as changing specific assumptions. But "kill fast" must be done on the right signal: in AI categories, early cohorts can be "tourist" cohorts, and average metrics distort the picture.

### Thesis A4: PM/founder becomes an operator of a hypothesis-testing factory (without an OS this turns into noise)
AI increases the number of testable hypotheses "per unit time." This makes the founder an operator of a conveyor: choosing hypotheses, designing tests, interpreting results, making decisions. But AI also changes workflow: people write more and delete more code, and productivity becomes multidimensional. So the hypothesis factory needs an operating system: assumption ledger, decision log, staged validation rules, and stop criteria.

### Thesis A5: PMF is not binary: focus on the killer assumption (cost of error x cost of test)
PMF is a continuum. At any given time you have the most dangerous risk that could kill the business, and it needs to be tested first. RAT formalizes priority: cost of error and cost of validation. But in AI categories there's a special risk — misinterpreting early metrics (AI tourists), so you need to define the signal horizon in advance (e.g., M3 retention) and pivot/iterate criteria.

### Thesis A6: Jobs are primary: the key mistake is ignoring segment and Job selection (AI prompts don't replace evidence)
The product exists to perform the customer's Job, and profit only comes if you create added value in a chosen segment. The most common mistake: skip segment selection and "best Job" choice and jump straight to building. AI can speed up segment/assumption generation, but increases the risk of "many words, little knowledge": you need to explicitly describe the process of turning LLM hypotheses into evidence (interviews, presale, cohorts).
