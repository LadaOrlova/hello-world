## TASK 0: Why Now?

Vibe-coding tools crossed the adoption threshold in late 2025 -- for the first time, a solo founder can ship a working product in a weekend, which means the constraint that kept teams honest (expensive code = forced validation) has evaporated, and without a new discipline the default behavior is "build more, learn less" at precisely the moment AI-category markets are rotating fast enough to invalidate PMF every quarter.

## TASK 1: Narrative Arc

### Conflict (What the reader believes now VS what the article will show)

**Reader's current belief:** "AI lets me build faster, so I'll find PMF faster. Speed of shipping = speed of winning."

**What the article will show:** Speed of building is no longer the constraint. Speed of *learning* is. Building faster without a system for validating and deciding actually makes you slower -- you generate more noise, test the wrong things, and mistake motion for progress. The bottleneck moved, and most teams are still optimizing the old one.

### Transformation

The reader enters as a builder ("I ship, therefore I learn") and exits as a *knowledge buyer* ("I test the right assumption this week, interpret the signal, and make a kill/iterate/pivot decision -- that's what progress looks like").

Behavioral change: after reading, the reader should be able to name their single riskiest assumption *right now* and design a test for it before Friday.

## TASK 2: Hook -- 3 Variants

### Variant A: Shock

> Last year a solo founder shipped an AI app in 72 hours using vibe-coding tools. It got 10,000 signups in a week. He thought he'd found product-market fit. By day ten the retention chart looked like a cliff -- 96% of users never came back. The product wasn't slow to build. It was fast to build and slow to learn. That gap is now the number-one killer of AI-era startups, and most teams don't even see it.

**Why it works:** Opens with a specific, concrete, visceral image -- the cliff chart. Delivers the core argument (build speed != learning speed) through a story the reader can *see*. The shock is that the very thing founders celebrate (speed) is the thing destroying them. The HackerNoon audience will recognize this pattern from their own feeds.

### Variant B: Story

> In 2016, Rik Higham published an article on HackerNoon arguing that the MVP is dead and founders should run Risk Assumption Tests instead. A decade later, AI has made his case even stronger -- but in reverse. The MVP used to be expensive, so teams were forced to validate before building. Now building is nearly free, and validation is the step everyone skips. The result: more products than ever, and the same 90% failure rate.

**Why it works:** Self-referential nod to HackerNoon's own publishing history (Rik Higham's 2016 article). Creates a "the more things change" tension. The reader who remembers that article will lean in; the reader who doesn't will learn something. The punchline ("same 90% failure rate") sets up the entire article's argument: cheap code didn't fix the real problem.

### Variant C: Tension (Contradiction)

> Here's the contradiction nobody in the vibe-coding community wants to talk about: AI made building a product nearly free -- and finding product-market fit just as hard as it ever was. The bottleneck didn't shrink. It moved. It used to be "can we build this?" Now it's "can we learn fast enough to know *what* to build?" Every founder who shipped a weekend prototype and called it PMF is about to discover the difference between a demo and a business.

**Why it works:** Leads with direct confrontation ("nobody wants to talk about"). Sets up an explicit paradox that the HackerNoon audience -- technical builders who love vibe-coding -- will feel personally. The last sentence ("the difference between a demo and a business") is the knife twist that earns the rest of the article.

## TASK 3: Section Order

### Proposed order (same as the brief's section map):

1. **Hook** (~120 words)
2. **Section 1: The Speed Trap** (A1 + N5 + N6 + N4) (~300 words)
3. **Section 2: Your Product Is Already Dead** (A3 + A6) (~300 words)
4. **Section 3: Find the Riskiest Assumption First** (A5 + N15 + N13) (~280 words)
5. **Section 4: The Hypothesis Factory OS** (A4 + N8 + N9 + N10 + N12) (~350 words)
6. **Section 5: Don't Trust Average Metrics** (A2 + N2 + N3 + N1 + N11) (~300 words)
7. **Close** (~150 words)

### Logic behind this order:

The article follows a **"destroy, then rebuild"** architecture -- a classic persuasion structure for HackerNoon's analytical audience.

**Phase 1 -- Destroy the old model (Sections 1-2):**
- S1 attacks the reader's *current behavior* (building fast = winning fast). This is the bait -- they click because they vibe-code and want to learn more, then get confronted with the trap.
- S2 attacks the reader's *mindset* (your goal is to launch a product). By now the reader is destabilized -- their tool (speed) is suspect, their goal (launch) is wrong. Maximum openness to a new model.

**Phase 2 -- Rebuild with a new system (Sections 3-4):**
- S3 gives the *compass* (which assumption to test first). This is the turning point -- the reader goes from doubt to "okay, tell me what to do."
- S4 gives the *operating system* (how to run the whole factory). This is the longest section because it's the highest-value: the reader walks away with a complete toolkit.

**Phase 3 -- Inoculate against the sneakiest trap (Section 5):**
- S5 is placed last in the body because it's the most *sophisticated* insight -- cohort noise, tourist metrics, PMF treadmill. Placing it earlier would overwhelm before the reader has the framework. Placing it last rewards the reader who stayed and gives them a "graduate-level" diagnostic they won't find elsewhere.

**Why this order is better than alternatives:**
- Starting with "Your Product Is Already Dead" (S2 first) was considered, but it's too confrontational as an opener without the Speed Trap setup -- the reader hasn't yet been shown *why* their current approach fails.
- Ending with "The Hypothesis Factory OS" (S4 last) was considered, but it's a "how-to" section -- better as mid-article where the reader is hungry for structure, not as a closer where you want emotional momentum.
- The metrics section (S5) as closer-adjacent creates a "trust but verify" ending: you have the system (S4), now here's where it can still go wrong (S5). That's the mark of a rigorous, non-superficial guide -- exactly what HackerNoon rewards.

## TASK 5: Close -- 3 Variants

### Variant A: Decision Reframe

> Here's what to do Monday morning. Open a blank doc. Write down every assumption your product depends on -- segment, job, value, channel, economics. Pick the one where being wrong costs the most and testing costs the least. Design a test. Run it this week. Write down the result and the decision: kill, iterate, or pivot. That's one cycle. Do it again next week. You're not launching a product. You're buying knowledge -- and the exchange rate just got a lot more favorable.

### Variant B: Provocation

> You shipped three features last week. How many assumptions did you test? If you can't answer that question instantly -- with the assumption named, the test described, and the decision recorded -- you're not running a hypothesis factory. You're running a feature mill. The code is cheap. Your judgment isn't. The fix isn't complicated. It's just uncomfortable.

### Variant C: Prediction

> In twelve months, two kinds of teams will exist. The first will have beautiful products, AI-generated at speed, with dashboards full of average metrics that say nothing -- and they'll be pivoting for the third time with no idea why the first two failed. The second will have an assumption ledger, a decision log, and a clear record of every bet they killed, iterated, or pivoted. They won't have shipped the most features. They'll have *learned* the most. The treadmill doesn't care which group you're in. It just keeps running.

## TASK 6: Memorable Moment

**Section: S3 -- Find the Riskiest Assumption First**

**The element:**

> "Every product has a killer assumption -- the single thing that, if wrong, makes everything else irrelevant. The segment could be perfect, the UX beautiful, the economics tight -- and if the killer assumption is false, the product is dead. Think of it like a murder mystery in reverse: you already know the victim (your idea). Your job is to find the murder weapon before the market does."

**Why this will stick:**
- The "murder mystery in reverse" metaphor is vivid, unexpected, and maps perfectly to the RAT framework.
- It gives the reader a mental image they can carry: every time they look at their product, they think "what's the murder weapon I haven't found yet?"
- It inverts the typical startup narrative (hero's journey) into a detective story -- and detective stories are about *what you don't know*, which is exactly the article's thesis.
- It aligns with Ivan's voice: concrete image first, then principle. Military/strategy adjacent. Short punchy sentence to land it.

## Recommendations for Writer Agent

1. **Default hook: Variant C (Tension).** It's the most aligned with HackerNoon's analytical audience and sets up the core paradox without requiring a specific case (keeping word budget tight).

2. **Default close: Variant B (Provocation).** Ivan's voice is at its strongest in direct confrontation. "You shipped three features last week. How many assumptions did you test?" is the kind of question that lives in a reader's head.

3. **The Memorable Moment (murder mystery in reverse) should be formatted as a standalone paragraph in S3** -- surrounded by whitespace, not buried in a list. It's the image the reader carries away.

4. **Section 4 is the make-or-break section.** If the reader skims, they'll skim here. Use a bullet list or numbered steps for the OS components (ledger, log, staged validation, stop criteria). The structure *is* the argument -- it needs to look systematic on the page.

5. **Do not summarize in the close.** The brief and Ivan's voice both demand forward motion. The close should feel like the start of something, not the end of an article.

6. **AI-detection risk management:** Keep sentence length variable (mix 5-word punches with 30-word compound sentences). Use Ivan's distinctive phrasings verbatim where possible. Avoid hedging language ("it could be argued," "one might consider"). Ivan doesn't hedge. He claims, then backs it up.
