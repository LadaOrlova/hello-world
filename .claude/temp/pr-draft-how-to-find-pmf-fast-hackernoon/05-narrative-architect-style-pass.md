## 1. HOOK OPTIONS (3 variants)

### Hook A: Sharpen Ivan's current hook for HN format

> Lovable hit $200M ARR in under a year — the fastest AI startup ever to cross $100M. You'd think that means product-market fit is locked down. Their Head of Growth, Elena Verna, calls PMF a "perishable good" — something the team re-earns every 90 days. The playbook of "find PMF, then scale" is a trap. This is the operating manual for the treadmill that never stops.

**Type:** Data hook + authority + metaphor.
**Why it works for HN:** Leads with a specific, impressive number ($200M ARR). Name-drops a recognized growth operator. The "perishable good" concept is counterintuitive and tweetable. Tighter than Ivan's original — cut from ~95 words to ~75 by removing the Dropbox/Miro/SurveyMonkey resume line (HN readers either know Verna or don't; the resume slows the hook without adding credibility for this audience).

**What changed vs. Ivan's original:** Removed the parenthetical bio of Verna. Removed "What used to be long-horizon innovation now expires in a single quarter" (redundant with "every 90 days"). Compressed two sentences into one at the end.

---

### Hook B: Contrarian/data lead

> Ninety percent of startups fail. That number hasn't changed in a decade — not even after AI made it possible to build a working product in a weekend. The bottleneck was never code. It was learning which assumptions would kill you before the market did. This article is a system for doing that faster.

**Type:** Startling-stat + contrarian reframe.
**Why it works for HN:** Opens with the stat HN readers have seen a hundred times, then immediately subverts it — "AI didn't fix this." The paradox (cheaper building, same failure rate) is the core tension of the article delivered in two sentences. The last line promises a system, which is what HN readers click for.

---

### Hook C: Direct challenge

> You shipped a prototype last weekend. AI wrote most of the code. You have signups. You think you have product-market fit. You don't — you have a demo with traffic. PMF is not a launch metric. It is a survival mechanism that Lovable, the fastest-growing AI startup in history, has to re-earn every 90 days. Here's how to stop confusing motion with progress.

**Type:** Second-person confrontation + authority anchor.
**Why it works for HN:** Speaks directly to the vibe-coding builder persona that dominates HN's readership right now. The "You shipped... You think... You don't" cadence creates escalating tension. Lands the Lovable data point as proof that even the best aren't safe. The last sentence frames the article as a diagnostic, not a lecture.

---

### RECOMMENDATION: Hook A

Hook A is the strongest choice for three reasons:

1. **It preserves Ivan's framing.** The Lovable/$200M/Verna setup is genuinely strong — it's specific, sourced, and counterintuitive. The problem with the current version is length, not concept. Hook A keeps the payload and cuts the fat.

2. **It matches HN's "show the math" pattern.** The style research shows HN's best hooks lead with a specific number. Hook A does this ($200M ARR, 90 days). Hook B uses a generic stat (90% failure) that HN readers have heard too many times. Hook C has no number in the opening sentence.

3. **It avoids AI-detection risk.** Hook C's "You... You... You..." cadence is a pattern AI detectors flag. Hook B's "The bottleneck was never X. It was Y." is another common AI structure. Hook A reads like a journalist wrote it — fact, context, twist, promise.

## 3. CLOSE OPTIONS

### Close A: Sharpen Ivan's current close

**Ivan's current close:**
> The 6-point checklist + "PMF is not something you find. It is something you keep finding."

**Proposed sharpened version:**

Keep the 6-point checklist exactly as-is (it's well-constructed — actionable, parallel, specific). Sharpen only the framing and the final line:

> **The Operating Checklist**
>
> 1. **Start with jobs and segments** — this is the root cause of everything else.
> 2. **List your riskiest assumptions.** Prioritize by: (probability of error x consequences) / cost of test.
> 3. **Use AI to build artifacts fast** — but measure progress in validated checks, not shipped features.
> 4. **Run the factory with an OS:** assumption ledger, decision log, staged validation, explicit stop criteria.
> 5. **When pivoting, name exactly which assumption changed** and why.
> 6. **Accept the treadmill.** Schedule the next round of re-validation before you need it.
>
> PMF is not something you find. It is something you keep finding. The only question is whether you have a system for it — or whether you're just running.

**What changed:** Added one sentence after the closing maxim. "The only question is whether you have a system for it — or whether you're just running" does two things: (a) reframes the treadmill metaphor one final time (running on the treadmill vs. running blind), and (b) gives the reader a binary self-diagnosis they'll carry away.

---

### Close B: Provocation close (alternative)

Replace everything after the 6-point checklist with:

> You shipped features last week. How many assumptions did you validate? If you can't answer that — with the assumption named, the test described, and the decision recorded — you're not running a hypothesis factory. You're running a feature mill on autopilot.
>
> PMF is not something you find. It is something you keep finding. Start with item one. Do it before Friday.

**Why this works:** Direct confrontation is Ivan's strongest register ("The fix isn't complicated. It's just uncomfortable."). The "How many assumptions did you validate?" question is the kind of thing a reader screenshots. "Do it before Friday" echoes Ivan's cadence pattern and creates immediate urgency. This close is higher-risk (some readers will feel attacked) but higher-reward (more memorable, more shareable).

---

### RECOMMENDATION: Close A

Close A is the safer and better-fitting choice for HackerNoon. Reasons:

1. The checklist is the article's highest-value asset — it's what readers bookmark. Close A lets it breathe. Close B adds confrontation that competes with the checklist for attention.

2. Close A's added sentence ("The only question is whether you have a system for it") reframes without attacking. HN's audience skews toward builders who are already trying hard — confrontation works in hooks (where you earn the right to teach), but in closes it can feel like a lecture.

3. Ivan's closing maxim ("PMF is not something you find. It is something you keep finding.") is already excellent — it's crisp, quotable, and structurally mirrors the article's argument. Both closes preserve it. Close A amplifies it; Close B buries it under a new provocation.
