## Section Brief: 1. The Speed Trap

**Theses:** A1 + N5 + N6 + N4
**Word budget:** ~300 words

### SECTION SKELETON (thesis body — verbatim):
AI lowers the prototyping barrier, so you can show people more "real" solutions more often and get objections/fears/value-fit signals faster. But code generation speed != knowledge acquisition speed: vibe-coding can be stochastic, and AI tools push toward "more code, less validation." The right algorithm: use AI as an artifact accelerator, but measure progress by the number of valid checks and signal quality. Validation bandwidth — how many valid checks you can run and correctly interpret per unit time — is the real bottleneck.

### MUST-INCLUDE ELEMENTS:

**DATA (mandatory numbers):**
1. METR study: developers 19% SLOWER with AI tools, but believed they were 24% FASTER (RCT, 2025)
2. Vibe-coding = "rolling the dice" — stochastic code generation (arXiv 2512.22418)

**CONCEPT (mandatory concepts):**
1. Vibe-coding stochasticity
2. "More code, less validation" — the main anti-pattern of cheap building
3. Validation bandwidth — the new bottleneck

**KB:**
1. > "The best way to validate a value hypothesis is through sales." — Source: `validate-value.md`

**COUNTER:**
1. METR (2025-07-10): AI may slow experienced developers while creating subjective feeling of acceleration — https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/

## Section Brief: 2. Your Product Is Already Dead

**Theses:** A3 + A6
**Word budget:** ~300 words

### SECTION SKELETON (thesis body — verbatim):
A new idea almost always rests on false assumptions. The right goal of the early stage is not "launch a product" but to buy knowledge about what exactly will kill it (market/segment/job/value/channel/economics), and to pivot as changing specific assumptions. The product exists to perform the customer's Job, and profit only comes if you create added value in a chosen segment. The most common mistake: skip segment selection and "best Job" choice and jump straight to building. AI can speed up segment/assumption generation, but increases the risk of "many words, little knowledge."

### MUST-INCLUDE ELEMENTS:

**DATA:**
1. 90-95% probability that your idea is already dead (author's frame)

**CONCEPT:**
1. "The idea is already dead" — reframe from "launch" to "find cause of death"

3. Jobs are primary — the product exists to perform the customer's Job
4. Segment + Job selection = the key strategic decision

## Section Brief: 3. Find the Riskiest Assumption First

**Theses:** A5 + N15 + N13
**Word budget:** ~280 words

### SECTION SKELETON (thesis body — verbatim):
PMF is a continuum. At any given time you have the most dangerous risk that could kill the business, and it needs to be tested first. RAT formalizes priority: cost of error and cost of validation. "Pivot vs optimize" needs explicit language: local optimum (improve current product: A/B tests, UX, +10-30%) vs global optimum (change segment, market, business model: high risk, x2-x10). Weekly focus: 1 killer assumption per week. Design the test, record the result, make the decision.

### MUST-INCLUDE ELEMENTS:

**DATA:**
1. RAT formula: cost of error x cost of test prioritization
2. Risk multiplication: 7 assumptions at 40% each = 0.3% survival (0.4^7)

**CONCEPT:**
1. RAT — Risk Assumption Test
2. Killer assumption — the one that could kill the business, test it first
3. "Killer assumption of the week" — practical focus unit
4. Local vs Global Optimum — pivot (global) vs optimize (local)

**KB:**
1. > "...we rank by risk (cost of error) and cost of validation, test the riskiest first." — Source: `risk-assumption-test.md`

## Section Brief: 4. The Hypothesis Factory OS

**Theses:** A4 + N8 + N9 + N10 + N12
**Word budget:** ~350 words

### SECTION SKELETON (thesis body — verbatim):
AI increases the number of testable hypotheses "per unit time." This makes the founder an operator of a conveyor: choosing hypotheses, designing tests, interpreting results, making decisions. The factory needs an operating system:
- Assumption ledger — tracking what's tested, what's disproven, what changes
- Decision log — every week ends with a decision (kill/iterate/pivot) and argument
- Staged validation: promise -> demo -> UX test -> cohorts -> channel scaling
- Stop criteria: no sales after 5 iterations -> error is likely in segment/job
Quality of learning > quantity of wins. KPI = knowledge, not artifacts.

### MUST-INCLUDE ELEMENTS:

**DATA:**
1. "More code + more deletion" in real developer logs (arXiv 2601.10258)
2. ~100 checks (interviews + solution + UX) = empirical average for PMF
3. Iterations of 6 with explicit stop criteria
4. No sales after 5 iterations -> fundamental error in segment/job

**CONCEPT:**
1. Hypothesis factory — PM as operator of a testing conveyor
2. Assumption ledger — tracking tested/disproven/changed
3. Decision log — weekly decisions with arguments
4. Staged validation: promise -> demo -> UX -> cohorts -> channel
5. Quality of learning > quantity of wins

## Section Brief: 5. Don't Trust Average Metrics

**Theses:** A2 + N2 + N3 + N1 + N11
**Word budget:** ~300 words

### SECTION SKELETON (thesis body — verbatim):
AI lets you quickly assemble and modify solution variants for different segments and Jobs — useful for divergence. But in consumer AI there's attention/subscription concentration: users often pick one main tool. Find the "foundational cohort" — the group that retains because the product hits their core workload. PMF is a treadmill in AI categories: fit must be re-found as models and expectations change. Average metrics across all users hide the foundational cohort. Early cohorts may be "tourists" — average metrics mislead. The right diagnostic: "who retained, what exactly are they doing, and why is it repeatable?"

### MUST-INCLUDE ELEMENTS:

**DATA:**
1. Consumer AI "winner-take-most": <10% cross-visit rate (a16z 2025)
2. 91% of consumers have only one AI subscription (a16z 2025)
3. "Glass slipper" cohort effect: Gemini 2.5 Pro retained ~35% at 5 months; later cohorts churned (a16z 2025-12-08)

**QUOTE:**
1. Elena Verna: "PMF used to be something companies would hit, maintain, and scale. That era is dead." — https://www.elenaverna.com/p/the-product-market-fit-treadmill

**CONCEPT:**
1. "Glass slipper" cohort — PMF can be cohort-level, not average
2. Foundational cohort — the group that retains because product hits core workload
3. Cohort noise — "tourists" vs core users distort early metrics
4. PMF treadmill — fit must be re-found
5. Consumer AI concentration — become "the default" for one workload

**KB:**
1. > "Chose the segment's job = chose your destiny." — Source: `segmentation.md`

### CASES:

LiFT media agency — focused on cheap "experts" segment (150K RUB/month). Course for experts flopped. After segmentation, discovered top-managers buy easier, churn less, value "done for you." Dropped average segment, focused on foundational cohort. Check x3 (150K->450K), retention 1 month -> 1 year, revenue +65%.
