## TASK 2: Extracted Stylistic Patterns

### 2.1 Structural Patterns

**Average article length:** ~2,300 words (range: 1,100–3,800)
- Short punchy pieces (1,100–1,200 words): opinion/critique format
- Medium how-to pieces (1,800–2,400 words): framework + examples
- Deep guides (3,500–3,800 words): comprehensive step-by-step
- **Our target:** 2,000–2,800 words (medium-to-deep range fits guide/framework format)

**Headline types observed:**
- Imperative/Warning: "Read This Before You Write Another Agent Skill"
- Numbered steps: "5 Steps To Achieving Product Market Fit And The 40% Rule"
- Contrarian: "Don't Just Talk to Your Users"
- Question-based: "Is Your Idea Worth Pursuing?"
- Dramatic statement: "The Most Painful Startup Failure: Loved Product, Zero Business"
- How-to + secret: "Unlock the Secret to Startup Success by Mastering the Explore-Exploit Loop"
- **Dominant pattern: Title contains a CONFLICT or CHALLENGE to conventional wisdom**

**Average paragraph length:** 2–4 sentences (40–80 words). No article uses long academic paragraphs. Short paragraphs dominate — some are single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.

**Use of bullet points/lists:** 7 of 8 articles use lists extensively. Lists appear in:
- Step breakdowns (numbered)
- Feature/criteria enumeration (bulleted)
- Summary/takeaway sections (bulleted)
- Tool stacks (bulleted)
- Only the personal narrative piece (Article 8, "Knowing When to Hard Pivot") uses pure prose without lists

**Subheading frequency:** Every 200–400 words. Average: 6–8 subheadings per article. Subheadings are action-oriented or question-based, not generic labels.

**Hook format (first 2–3 paragraphs):**
- Pattern A (most common): Open with a STARTLING FACT or STATISTIC, then pose the question the article answers. Example: "Over 90% of startups fail... Why?"
- Pattern B: Open with PERSONAL STORY of failure/pivot, then promise the framework. Example: "In mid-May, we were just closing our seed round... Two weeks later, we started to have concerns."
- Pattern C: Challenge a popular belief directly. Example: "Most people can more or less agree on how it feels to have PMF, but not on what it really is."
- **NO article opens with a generic "In today's fast-paced world" or "As we all know" preamble.**

**Conclusion format:**
- Pattern A: Actionable summary with 3–5 bullet takeaways
- Pattern B: Reframe of the opening question + single memorable line. Example: "Companies that don't make money are just an elaborate hobby."
- Pattern C: Promise of what comes next (series articles)
- **Every conclusion is SHORT (1–3 paragraphs max). No article repeats the whole piece in the conclusion.**

### 2.2 Tonal Patterns

**Formality scale:** 4/10 (conversational-professional)
- Not academic, not slang
- Reads like a smart colleague explaining over coffee
- Technical precision on concepts, casual in connecting sentences

**First person usage:** Yes, in 7 of 8 articles. Used for:
- Establishing authority: "I've seen this pattern in..."
- Sharing experience: "We had enough cash for 2 years and decided to pivot"
- Expressing opinion: "I would argue..." / "In my opinion..."

**Direct address ("you"):** Present in ALL 8 articles. "You" is the default mode of addressing the reader. Often combined with rhetorical questions: "So what do you do with this information?"

**Humour/irony:** Mild and purposeful. Not jokes, but wry observations:
- "Companies that don't make money are just an elaborate hobby — and there are much better hobbies than running a SaaS company."
- "Happy Hacking." (sign-off)
- Never forced, never slapstick

**Expert vs. conversational tone:** Both, simultaneously. The pattern is:
1. State a framework or concept with authority
2. Immediately soften with a personal anecdote or casual aside
3. Return to authority with data or examples

### 2.3 Content Patterns

**Data/statistics density:** Medium. Key pattern:
- 2–5 specific data points per article (not more)
- Numbers used as HOOKS (opening) and PROOF (mid-article), never as decoration
- Precise numbers preferred: "$1.1 billion acquisition" not "about a billion"
- Percentages used for emphasis: "90% of startups fail," "40% rule"
- Financial metrics common: ARR, LTV:CAC ratios, acquisition prices

**Expert quote format:**
- Quotes are RARE in HackerNoon articles (only 2 of 8 articles use direct quotes)
- When used, they reference well-known frameworks (Andy Rachleff, Paul Graham) not random experts
- Format: paraphrase the concept, then attribute. Not "As X said, '...'" but "The concept, first articulated by X, suggests that..."
- HackerNoon's own guide warns: quotes-as-filler is a pitfall to avoid

**Case study format:**
- 3 of 8 articles use case studies as primary structure
- Pattern: Company name as subheading → 1 paragraph context → 2–3 paragraphs of what happened → 1 paragraph lesson
- Real companies preferred (Slack, MoviePass, Tumblr, HQ Trivia)
- Specific metrics always accompany case studies

**Actionable advice format:**
- Numbered steps (most common): "Step 1... Step 2..."
- Framework presentation: Name the framework → explain → show application
- "Do this, not that" contrasts: "Don't just talk to users" → "Interview 3 groups instead"
- Checklists with criteria (e.g., evaluation templates)

**Frameworks/models used:**
- Every successful article introduces or applies at least one named framework
- Examples: 40% Rule, Vitamin vs. Advil, Explore-Exploit Loop, Business Model Canvas, ICE Scoring, Jobs-to-be-Done, The Mom Test, Multi-Armed Bandit
- Framework-driven articles rank highest in search results

### 2.4 Viral/Citable Traits

**What type of conflict opens the best pieces?**
1. **"Everyone does X, but X is wrong"** — contrarian openings dominate. Challenge a popular belief immediately.
2. **"We had success and it still failed"** — paradox of partial success (e.g., loved product + zero business)
3. **"Here's the stat that should scare you"** — startling data hook (90% fail rate)

**What headline format gets most traction?**
1. **"How to [ACTION] + [QUALIFIER]"** — "How To Do It Right," "How to Find Out"
2. **"[NUMBER] Steps/Ways + [DESIRED OUTCOME]"** — "5 Steps To Achieving PMF"
3. **"[CONTRARIAN CLAIM]: [SUBVERSION]"** — "Don't Just Talk to Your Users"
4. Headlines under 80 characters outperform longer ones (HackerNoon editorial rule)

**What increases quotability?**
1. **Crisp maxims** embedded in the text: "Companies that don't make money are just an elaborate hobby"
2. **Named frameworks** readers can reference: "the 40% rule," "Vitamin vs. Advil"
3. **Counterintuitive data pairs**: "73 million users, $13 million revenue"
4. **Concrete contrasts**: before/after, old way/new way

## TASK 3: Supplementary Style Guide

### SUPPLEMENTARY STYLE GUIDE for HackerNoon
*(These rules apply WHERE the media brief is silent. Media brief always takes priority.)*

**Headlines:** 60–80 characters. Must contain either a number, a contrarian claim, or a "how to" + qualifier. Avoid generic nouns; use specific outcomes. Title case. No clickbait — HackerNoon editors explicitly reject it. Test: "Would I click this on Twitter?"

**Hook (first 100 words):** Open with ONE of three patterns: (a) a startling statistic, (b) a personal failure/pivot story, or (c) a direct challenge to conventional wisdom. NEVER open with "In today's fast-paced world" or any variant. The first sentence must create tension or curiosity. Second paragraph must promise what the reader will learn.

**Paragraphs:** 2–4 sentences max (40–80 words). Use single-sentence paragraphs sparingly for emphasis. Break any paragraph longer than 5 sentences.

**Subheadings:** Every 200–400 words. Format: action-oriented or question-based. Use H2 for main sections, H3 for sub-steps. Examples: "Why Talking to Users Isn't Enough" (not "User Research"). "Step 3: Validate With Real Money" (not "Validation").

**Tone:** Conversational authority — formality level 4/10. Write like a smart colleague, not a professor. Use first person ("I") for experience, second person ("you") for advice. Mild irony is welcome; forced jokes are not. Be direct and assertive in opinions.

**Lists:** Use numbered lists for sequential steps/processes. Use bullet points for non-sequential items (criteria, tools, examples). Lists appear in 87% of top HackerNoon articles. Keep list items to 1–2 sentences each.

**Expert quotes:** Use sparingly (max 1–2 per article). Only quote recognized authorities (Rachleff, Graham, Blank). Paraphrase first, then attribute — never lead with "As X said..." Avoid filler quotes entirely.

**Data points:** 3–6 specific data points per article. Use precise numbers ("$1.1 billion" not "about a billion"). Place the strongest stat in the opening hook. Use data as PROOF, not decoration. Pair numbers with context: "3 million users — but each one cost 10x the subscription price."

**Conclusion:** SHORT — 1–3 paragraphs maximum. Either (a) a 3–5 bullet actionable summary or (b) a single memorable reframe of the opening tension. End with a crisp maxim or a forward-looking statement. Never rehash the entire article.

**Named frameworks:** Introduce or apply at least ONE named framework with a memorable label. Framework-driven articles consistently rank highest on HackerNoon. The name should be catchy and self-explanatory (e.g., "the 40% rule," "Vitamin vs. Advil").

**Case studies:** Include 2–3 real company examples. Format: company name → 1 paragraph context → 2–3 paragraphs detail → 1 paragraph lesson. Always pair with specific metrics.

**AVOID:**
- Generic openings ("In today's fast-paced world...")
- Passive voice (HackerNoon editorial: "Microsoft slashed prices" beats "Prices were cut by Microsoft")
- Paragraphs longer than 5 sentences
- Quotes used as filler
- Surface-level content without concrete detail, steps, or data
- "Search engine trap" — writing for SEO instead of readers
- Unattributed claims presented as fact
- Abstract advice without examples ("be customer-centric" — say HOW)
- Overly polished, uniform tone that signals AI generation

### TOP-3 TECHNIQUES from the best articles:

**1. "Paradox Hook" — Open with a contradiction that creates tension**
From "The Most Painful Startup Failure": The article opens by establishing that PMF is the holy grail, then immediately asks "But when you start looking into it, there are a lot of exceptions. Why?" This creates a gap between what readers believe and what the article reveals. Technique: State the accepted wisdom in one sentence. Subvert it in the next.

**2. "Named Framework as Anchor" — Give your method a memorable name**
From "5 Steps to Achieving PMF and the 40% Rule": The "40% rule" appears in the headline and serves as a concrete, testable metric readers can immediately apply. From "Knowing When to Hard Pivot": "Vitamin vs. Advil" is a two-word framework that reframes the entire PMF discussion. Technique: Distill your core insight into a 2–4 word phrase that readers can repeat in conversation.

**3. "Show the Math, Not the Theory" — Anchor abstractions in specific numbers**
From "The Most Painful Startup Failure": MoviePass — "$10/month subscription but each user cost 10x that in theater payouts." Tumblr — "73 million users, $1.1B acquisition, $13M revenue." The numbers do the arguing, not the author. Technique: For every abstract claim, provide one specific data point that makes the reader stop and re-read.

### VIRAL TRIGGERS to replicate:

**1. "Success that was actually failure" — The false-positive narrative**
Example: "MoviePass had 3 million paying users and was bankrupt." / "We had 350k ARR and still needed to pivot." This trigger works because it challenges the reader's internal metric for success. Readers click because they fear they might be in the same trap.
→ *For our article:* Open with a company that seemed to have PMF by all standard metrics but was building on sand. Frame PMF as something most founders think they have — but don't.

**2. "The counterintuitive instruction" — Tell them NOT to do the popular thing**
Example: "Don't Just Talk to Your Users" / "Read This Before You Write Another Agent Skill." The trigger is the cognitive dissonance: every startup guide says "talk to users," and this title says the opposite. Readers click to resolve the tension.
→ *For our article:* Consider a subheading like "Stop Building Features — Start Running Experiments" or "Why Customer Love Doesn't Mean Product-Market Fit."

**3. "The specific number as proof of authority" — Quantified credibility**
Example: "21 customer interviews in the first month" / "80% of startups don't find PMF" / "40% rule." Specific numbers signal that the author has done the work, not just theorized. They also create shareability — "Did you know 80% of startups never find PMF?" is a tweetable fact.
→ *For our article:* Embed 4–5 specific, sourced data points. Lead with the most surprising one. Ensure at least one number appears in the headline or subtitle.

### HACKERNOON-SPECIFIC PLATFORM RULES (from editorial docs):

1. **AI detection:** HackerNoon tracks AI-generated content. Their Feb 2026 article "Beyond the Bots" explicitly says: "Never let AI write your lead or quotes. That's where your story's soul lives." Use AI as a tool, but the voice, opening, and key arguments must sound distinctly human.

2. **Three irreplaceable elements** (per HackerNoon's own framework):
   - **Taste:** Your judgment about what to cover and which details matter
   - **Reporting:** Actual friction — calls, tests, research beyond public information
   - **Point of view:** Interpretation for readers who lack context

3. **3-Act Structure** (from "Technical Detail Is How You Reach Our Readers in 2026"):
   - Act 1 (Hook): Establish why this matters and what readers gain
   - Act 2 (The How): Detailed walkthrough — the bulk of the article
   - Act 3 (Wrap-up): Synthesize lessons, provide actionable takeaways

4. **Submission checklist:** Featured image, meta description, TL;DR section, up to 8 tags

5. **Headline rule:** Max 80 characters. Must pass: "Would you click it on Twitter?" No clickbait. Use title case.

6. **Read-time metric:** HackerNoon measures success by minutes spent reading, not clicks. Write for engagement depth, not just headline appeal.

7. **Verbs matter:** Active voice is a firm editorial preference. "Microsoft slashed prices" not "Prices were cut by Microsoft."
