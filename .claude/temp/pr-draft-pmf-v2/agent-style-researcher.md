## 2. Extracted Stylistic Patterns

### 2A. Structural Patterns

**Length distribution (articles in the PMF/startup niche):**
- 6 of 8 articles fall in the 1,800-2,800 word range
- Sweet spot for the niche: 1,800-2,500 words
- The 8,500-word outlier (Renjit Philip) used a day-by-day diary format -- an exception, not the norm
- Recommendation for our article: target 1,500-1,800 words (media brief says 1,000-2,000; best performers cluster at the upper end)

**Headline formula:**
- Dominant pattern: "How to [Verb] [Object] [Qualifier]" -- 5 of 8 titles follow this
- Second pattern: provocative contrarian claim ("Growth Hacking Is Dead", "Most PMF Problems Are Actually QA Problems")
- Numbers in titles perform well ("5 Steps", "30 Days", "$127", "40%")
- HackerNoon official guideline: max 80 characters, Title Case, must pass the "Twitter test" (would someone click?)

**Subheadings:**
- Average: 5-8 subheadings per article (H2 level)
- Subheadings serve as a scannable table of contents
- Most popular subheading styles:
  - Numbered steps ("Step 1: ...", "Step 2: ...")
  - Question-form ("Why does PMF matter?", "Why Quality Gets Ignored")
  - Action-oriented imperative ("Start from a Minimum Viable Market", "Define Your Target Audience")
- Sub-subheadings (H3) used sparingly, only in longer articles

**Paragraphs:**
- Short paragraphs dominate: 2-4 sentences is the mode
- Single-sentence paragraphs used for emphasis (every article does this)
- No paragraph exceeds 6-7 sentences in any analyzed article
- Dense analytical paragraphs always followed by a visual break (list, bold statement, or whitespace)

**Lists and bullets:**
- Present in 100% of analyzed articles
- Average: 3-5 distinct list blocks per article
- Used for: step-by-step instructions, criteria enumeration, quick takeaways, mistake summaries
- Numbered lists for sequential processes; bullet points for non-ordered items

**Opening hook (first 3-4 sentences):**
- Pattern A -- Narrative scenario: "A VC leaned forward...", "In the beginning of June 2020, we had enough cash for 2 years and decided to pivot"
- Pattern B -- Provocative statement: "For a long time, growth hacking felt like a cheat code", "The death of most early-stage companies starts like this"
- Pattern C -- Relatable problem: "Imagine you have a business idea and it seems very good and feasible"
- Never starts with a definition. Never opens with "In this article, I will..."
- Hook length: 2-4 sentences maximum before transitioning to the problem

**Conclusion/closing:**
- Actionable summary in 2-3 sentences (not a fluffy motivational paragraph)
- Often circles back to the opening hook or metaphor
- Frequent pattern: "Until you [do X], you cannot [achieve Y]"
- Some articles end with a direct question to the reader ("What do you think?")
- No article ends with "In conclusion..." or "To sum up..."

**Visual elements:**
- Feature image required (HackerNoon editorial rule)
- Diagrams, screenshots, and infographics common in 5 of 8 articles
- Images serve functional purpose (illustrating a framework, showing a screenshot) rather than decoration

### 2B. Tonal Patterns

**Formality level: 6/10 (slightly informal professional)**
- Not academic, not slang
- Contractions used freely ("don't", "it's", "you're")
- Occasional colloquialisms ("cheat code", "kills it", "the vibes")
- Technical terms used but always explained or contextually obvious

**First person:**
- 7 of 8 articles use first person
- HackerNoon's official guideline: "Write in first person (I), not corporate 'we'"
- First person establishes credibility: "In my experience...", "I've observed..."
- Even framework-heavy articles ground theory in personal observation

**Direct address to reader ("you"):**
- Present in 100% of analyzed articles
- Creates intimacy and makes advice feel personalized
- Typical: "If you're building...", "Here's what you need to know"
- Rhetorical questions to the reader: "Why does this happen?", "But how do you know which features?"

**Expert vs. conversational balance:**
- Best-performing articles position the author as a "been there, done that" practitioner, not an ivory-tower theorist
- Self-deprecating humor appears in the most engaging pieces (Renjit Philip, Ivan Kuznetsov)
- Vulnerability about failures and mistakes builds trust
- Pattern: state something confidently, then immediately ground it in a concrete example

**Sentence rhythm:**
- Short declarative sentences for impact: "This doesn't work and they die."
- Followed by medium explanatory sentences (15-25 words)
- Long sentences (25+ words) rare and only for nuanced points
- Deliberate variation in sentence length prevents monotony

### 2C. Content Patterns

**Data density:**
- 6 of 8 articles include specific numbers/statistics
- Data used as proof points, not decoration: "90% of startups fail", "$127 total", "23 interested founders"
- Percentages and dollar amounts more impactful than abstract metrics
- Even articles without formal research cite specific thresholds ("40% rule", "3:1 LTV:CAC")
- Best practice: 3-5 concrete data points per 2,000-word article

**Case studies and examples:**
- 7 of 8 articles include at least one example
- Range: personal experience (most common), named companies (Slack, Airbnb, Amazon, Loom, Ahrefs), or hypothetical scenarios
- Personal case studies more engaging than third-party citations on HackerNoon
- Pattern: introduce a principle, then immediately show it in action with a specific example

**Frameworks and models:**
- 8 of 8 articles introduce or reference at least one framework
- Most popular: numbered step processes (3-step, 5-step)
- Named frameworks boost credibility: "Sean Ellis 40% test", "Business Model Canvas", "The Mom Test"
- HackerNoon readers expect systematized thinking, not just anecdotes

**Actionable advice:**
- Every article ends with something the reader can do immediately
- Checklists, step-by-step processes, and "if I started over" sections most common
- The more specific the advice, the better ("Interview 15+ potential customers", "Spend 1-2 hours on prioritization")
- Vague advice ("be customer-centric") never appears in top articles

**Attribution and sources:**
- HackerNoon guideline: "Share and attribute wisdom from other writers and thinkers"
- Books and thought leaders referenced naturally: "The Mom Test" by Rob Fitzpatrick, Teresa Torres' "Continuous Discovery Habits", Sean Ellis
- Links embedded naturally in text (never bare URLs -- official HackerNoon rule)

### 2D. Viral / Citable Traits

**Contrarian framing:**
- 4 of 8 articles challenge a conventional belief in the title or opening
- "Growth Hacking Is Dead", "Most PMF Problems Are Actually QA Problems", "Your product is already dead, you just don't know what will kill it"
- This is the single strongest pattern for engagement on HackerNoon

**Quotable one-liners:**
- Best articles contain 2-3 standalone sentences that work as social media quotes
- Examples: "Growth hacking chased attention. Systems earn it.", "Until you have 'correctly' built what you set out to build, it's tough to determine if your product is on the right track"
- Typically placed at the end of a section, not buried in a paragraph

**The "aha" moment:**
- Every top article delivers one reframe that changes how the reader thinks
- Example: "PMF is not binary -- it's a gradient" or "Quality problems masquerade as PMF problems"
- This reframe typically appears in the first third of the article (after the hook, before the framework)

**Shareability hooks:**
- Specific numbers in titles (30 days, $127, 5 steps, 40%)
- Paradox or tension ("burned everything and still found PMF")
- Implicit promise of a shortcut or insider knowledge
- Lists and step counts signal "I'll get something concrete from reading this"

## 4. Supplementary Style Rules

These rules address areas where the media brief is silent. They are derived from pattern analysis of top HackerNoon articles and official editorial guidelines. **They supplement, never override, the media brief.**

### 4.1 Headline Construction
- Keep under 80 characters (HackerNoon rule)
- Use Title Case
- Include one of: a number, a contrarian claim, or a specific mechanism
- Must work as a standalone tweet/social share
- Avoid question-form titles (they underperform on HackerNoon vs. declarative/how-to)

### 4.2 Opening Hook (First 100 Words)
- Open with a narrative micro-scene OR a provocative claim -- never with a definition or "In this article..."
- Ground the hook in a specific, tangible detail (a dollar amount, a conversation, a moment)
- Transition to the core problem within 3-4 sentences
- The media brief calls for a "short hook (3-4 sentences)" -- match this exactly

### 4.3 Author Voice and Positioning
- Write in first person ("I") per HackerNoon rules -- never "we" unless quoting a team experience
- Include 1-2 sentences early on establishing author credentials (e.g., "In my work across 80+ product teams..." or "After training 12,000+ product managers...")
- Use the media brief's recommended framing phrases: "In my experience across multiple product teams...", "A recurring pattern I've observed..."
- Maintain practitioner tone: someone who has done this, not someone who read about it

### 4.4 Paragraph Discipline
- Maximum 4 sentences per paragraph (hard ceiling)
- Use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis, but no more than 1 per section
- After every dense analytical paragraph, provide a visual break (list, bold statement, or subheading)
- No wall-of-text blocks -- scanability is critical for HackerNoon's readership

### 4.5 Subheading Style
- 5-7 subheadings for a 1,500-2,000 word article
- Mix of formats: numbered steps for the core algorithm, question-form for problem framing, imperative for action sections
- Subheadings should form a coherent "story arc" when read alone (a reader scanning only headings should understand the argument)

### 4.6 Data and Evidence
- Include 3-5 specific data points (numbers, percentages, dollar amounts)
- Cite sources for statistics (named studies, named experts, or "in my experience with N teams/products")
- Prefer concrete thresholds over vague claims: "90% of startups fail" > "most startups fail"
- At least one data point should be surprising or counterintuitive

### 4.7 Examples and Case Studies
- Include 2-3 examples minimum
- At least one should be personal/first-hand experience (matches HackerNoon's first-person preference)
- Frame case studies as evidence for a principle, not as self-promotion
- Use the media brief's non-promotional phrasings: "One example illustrates how this works in practice...", "Here's a specific mechanism that consistently produced results..."

### 4.8 Frameworks and Systematization
- The core argument must be presented as a system, algorithm, or numbered process (media brief: "algorithmicity")
- Name the framework or method explicitly (named frameworks are more citable and memorable)
- Keep the core framework to 3-5 steps (the dominant pattern on HackerNoon)
- Each step should have a clear input, action, and output

### 4.9 Sentence-Level Style
- Use contractions freely ("don't", "isn't", "you're")
- Vary sentence length deliberately: short punch (5-8 words) followed by medium explanation (15-20 words)
- Avoid passive voice unless emphasizing the object ("The product was killed by..." is acceptable if the death is the point)
- No jargon without immediate context (assume smart reader, not domain expert)
- Avoid AI-detectable patterns: no "delve", "leverage", "in the ever-evolving landscape", "it's important to note"

### 4.10 Quotable Lines
- Craft 2-3 sentences per article that work as standalone social media quotes
- Place them at section transitions (end of one section, just before the next subheading)
- Format: short, declarative, slightly provocative, contains the article's core reframe
- These should be the sentences that make a reader stop, screenshot, and share

### 4.11 Conclusion Style
- 2-4 sentences maximum
- Restate the core reframe (the "aha" moment) in its most compressed form
- End with either: a specific next action for the reader, or a forward-looking provocation
- Never end with "In conclusion..." or generic motivational language
- Optionally circle back to the opening hook for narrative closure

### 4.12 Formatting and Visual Hierarchy
- Bold key terms and framework names on first use
- Use numbered lists for sequential processes, bullet points for non-ordered items
- Include at least one visual element (diagram, framework visualization, or table) if possible
- No emoji in body text (HackerNoon's top articles avoid them, and they trigger AI-detection flags)

### 4.13 Anti-AI-Detection Measures
- The media brief warns: "AI text tracked -- ensure human voice"
- Use irregular sentence structures (fragments, interrupted thoughts, asides in dashes)
- Include specific first-hand observations that only a practitioner would know
- Vary paragraph length unpredictably (2 sentences, then 4, then 1, then 3)
- Use occasional colloquialisms or domain-specific slang that AI rarely generates
- Reference specific, named experiences ("When I worked with a B2B SaaS team in 2023..." not "In my experience with teams...")
- Avoid perfectly parallel structures (AI loves parallel lists; humans are messier)

## 5. Comparative Summary: What Top Articles Do vs. Don't Do

### Top articles DO:
- Open with a specific, tangible hook (scene, number, or contrarian claim)
- Present knowledge as a system with named steps
- Ground every principle in a concrete example
- Write in first person with practitioner authority
- Use short paragraphs and frequent visual breaks
- Include 3-5 hard data points
- Craft 2-3 quotable standalone sentences
- End with a specific, actionable takeaway

### Top articles DO NOT:
- Open with definitions or "In this article, I will..."
- Use generic motivational language ("believe in yourself", "the sky's the limit")
- Present advice without evidence or examples
- Write in corporate "we" voice
- Use long, unbroken paragraphs
- Include vague recommendations ("be customer-focused")
- Overuse formatting (no excessive bold, no emoji in body text)
- End with "In conclusion" or a summary of the summary

## 6. Recommended Structural Template for "How to Find PMF Fast"

Based on patterns from top HackerNoon articles, aligned with the media brief:

```
1. HOOK (3-4 sentences)
   - Narrative micro-scene or provocative claim
   - Specific detail (number, conversation, moment)
   - Transition to: "here's what's actually changing"

2. THE PROBLEM (200-300 words)
   - Current broken approach to PMF search
   - Why it fails (cause-effect chain)
   - Data point establishing the stakes

3. THE REFRAME / "AHA" MOMENT (150-200 words)
   - The core contrarian insight
   - [Quotable line here]
   - Why this changes everything

4. THE ALGORITHM / FRAMEWORK (500-700 words, core of the article)
   - Named framework with 3-5 numbered steps
   - Each step: what to do, why, example
   - At least 1 personal case study embedded

5. EXAMPLES IN ACTION (200-300 words)
   - 1-2 concrete applications of the framework
   - Non-promotional framing per media brief
   - Specific results or observations

6. ACTIONABLE SUMMARY (100-150 words)
   - Core reframe restated in compressed form
   - 1-2 specific next actions for the reader
   - Optional callback to the opening hook
```
