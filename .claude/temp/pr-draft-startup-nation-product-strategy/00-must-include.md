## Thesis 1: Product strategy = choosing which customer jobs you compete for. Getting it wrong is the #1 cause of failure.

### BODY (section skeleton — COPY IN FULL):
Everything in a product — value, features, landing pages, acquisition, retention — flows from knowing your customers' jobs. A rigid causal chain exists: Market → Segments → Jobs → Value → Communication → Conversion → Profit. An error at any earlier step breaks everything downstream. The vast majority of startups choose small, difficult, less profitable segments. Three catastrophic errors: (1) Product for non-existent jobs of non-existent people — most common cause of failure. (2) Working on unprofitable segments — a segment several times larger may exist nearby. (3) Spreading resources — features for everyone = value for no one. CB Insights (431 startups): 43% of failures = poor product-market fit.

- [ ] April Dunford: 40% of B2B deals lost to status quo, not competitors

## Thesis 2: Companies compete for the wrong segment, the wrong jobs — and pay for it with missed opportunity.

### BODY (section skeleton — COPY IN FULL):
Companies choose among customers who have many jobs, and the product competes for a small number of them — often not the most optimal. A larger, more solvent segment with higher-frequency jobs may exist nearby — but the founder doesn't know about it. Trying to serve two conflicting jobs simultaneously ("bipolar product problem") tears the product apart: InVideo spent 3 years serving beginners and experts → after JTBD-driven rejection of experts, growth from $0 to $25M in 6 months. Every time a founder builds a feature instead of seeing the full job graph, they incur opportunity cost.

### DATA (numbers and facts):
- [ ] InVideo: $0 → $25M in 6 months after dropping second segment
- [ ] Cockroach Labs: only 5-6 customer commitments in 5 years

### QUOTE (expert quotes):
- [ ] Bob Moesta: "You end up building a product that tries to do everything for everybody, and it ends up doing nothing for nobody." — [URL](https://saasclub.io/podcast/jobs-to-be-done-bob-moesta-423/)
- [ ] Shreyas Doshi: Opportunity cost thinking > ROI thinking — [URL](https://www.lennysnewsletter.com/p/episode-3-shreyas-doshi)
- [ ] Nate Stewart: "Customer commits = tech debt for product teams." — [URL](https://review.firstround.com/how-product-strategy-fails-in-the-real-world-what-to-avoid-when-building-highly-technical-products/)

### CONCEPT (frameworks and concepts):

## Thesis 3: There are ~80 product strategy mechanics — and each one changes the game.

### BODY (section skeleton — COPY IN FULL):
Key mechanics: (1) Higher-level job — most powerful (Yandex.Rent killed jobs "photograph apartment", "deal with tenants"). (2) Up-market shift — LiFT: check x3 when changing segment. (3) Previous job — Pleada: ROMI 800%→1200%. (4) Next job — cross-sell. (5) Kill the job — Uber killed "look for change", AirPods killed "untangle headphones". Full AURA methodology has ~80 strategies.

## Thesis 4: Case — JetBrains/Kotlin Multiplatform

### BODY (section skeleton — COPY IN FULL):
JetBrains Kotlin Multiplatform: hundreds of unresolved issues, demographic segmentation useless. JTBD segmentation revealed 2 key jobs: (1) reducing errors in business logic through shared code, (2) preserving native UI. Competitors focused on "release velocity" — KMP found a different angle. Result: top-3 cross-platform technologies from scratch, +20% satisfaction.
