### THESIS 1: Product strategy = choosing which customer jobs you compete for. Getting it wrong is the #1 cause of failure.

#### Case 1A: ArtDent (Dentistry, Nizhny Novgorod) -- RECOMMENDED FOR ARTICLE

**What happened:** ArtDent dental clinic grew intuitively to 30M+ rubles/month but plateaued. After 22 AJTBD interviews, they identified the most profitable segment: "complete dental repair in one day" (ticket 300-400K rubles, highest margin). They rebuilt their product, advertising, and lab partnerships around this one segment.

**Result:** Conversion ad-to-visit: 42% to 51%. Conversion visit-to-sale: 49% to 57%. Revenue: 38-39M to 53M/month (+37%).

For example, ArtDent dental clinic ran 22 customer interviews and discovered their highest-margin segment was 'fix all my teeth in one day.' By rebuilding everything around that one job, revenue jumped 37%." -- **PASS: This is direct proof that choosing the right job drives revenue.**

**PERCEPTION CHECK:** Reads as proof of a universal principle (specific numbers, recognizable business type, the insight is about the methodology, not about Ivan). Safe for solo use.

#### Case 1B: Legal Resources (LegalTech)

**What happened:** Legal Resources spent 6 years without profit, generating only 600K/month revenue while serving 500 companies and fulfilling any client request. After 15-17 deep AJTBD interviews (2-3 sessions of 1.5 hours each), they focused on the top-20 enterprise clients (>10K applications/month) and replaced manual legal work (1 lawyer = 1K applications) with product automation (50K applications/month).

**Result:** Revenue x13 (600K to 8M/month). First profit in 6 years. 3% market share in 2.5 months. 93% meeting-to-deal conversion.

### THESIS 2: Companies compete for the wrong segment, the wrong jobs -- and pay for it with missed opportunity.

#### Case 2A: LiFT (Media Agency) -- RECOMMENDED FOR ARTICLE (Thesis 3 mechanic)

**What happened:** LiFT media agency was doing "everything for everyone" with an average monthly fee of 150K rubles. They unknowingly focused on cheap expert clients. A course for experts flopped. **Result:** Average ticket x3 (150K to 450K/month). Client lifetime: 1 month to 1 year. Revenue +65%.

### THESIS 2: Wrong segment = missed opportunity

#### External Case 2A: InVideo -- $0 to $25M in 6 months (JTBD pivot) -- RECOMMENDED FOR ARTICLE

**What happened:** InVideo spent 3 years serving two conflicting segments: video beginners who wanted simplicity, and experts who wanted complexity. Bob Moesta calls this the "bipolar product problem" -- every feature pleased one half and enraged the other. After JTBD interviews revealed the conflict, InVideo abandoned the expert segment entirely, stripped the product down for beginners, and removed complexity features like timelines.

**Result:** $0 to $25M revenue within 6 months of the pivot.

**URL (verified):** [SaaS Club Podcast: Bob Moesta on JTBD](https://saasclub.io/podcast/jobs-to-be-done-bob-moesta-423/)

**Bob Moesta direct quote:** "Within six months, they went from zero to 25 million in revenue just by focusing on that one place."

**ARGUMENT TEST:** "Companies compete for the wrong segment and pay for it. For example, InVideo wasted 3 years trying to please both beginners and experts. After dropping experts entirely, they hit $25M in 6 months." -- **PASS: 3 years of wrong segment vs. 6 months of right segment is devastating proof of opportunity cost.**

---

#### External Case 2B: Cockroach Labs -- Refusing revenue to protect strategic focus

**What happened:** When Cockroach Labs was pre-revenue, potential customers offered $100K-$200K deals conditional on building analytics capabilities. Despite the pressure of zero revenue, the team refused. Nate Stewart (CPO): "Customer commits are tech debt for product teams. You limit your flexibility -- and what's more valuable to a startup than the ability to change course quickly?" In 5 years, they made only 5-6 customer commitments total.

**Result:** Maintained strategic focus on transactional databases. Successfully built a $3B+ company.

**URL (verified):** [First Round Review: How Product Strategy Fails](https://review.firstround.com/how-product-strategy-fails-in-the-real-world-what-to-avoid-when-building-highly-technical-products/)

**ARGUMENT TEST:** "The wrong job costs you missed opportunity. For example, Cockroach Labs refused $200K deals that would have pulled them toward the wrong job. In 5 years, they made only 6 customer commitments. That discipline built a $3B company." -- **PASS: The inverse case -- proving the thesis by showing what happens when you DON'T take the wrong job.**

### THESIS 5: PMF Decay -- 90-day expiration date

#### External Case 5A: Lovable -- $200M ARR company re-earning PMF every 90 days -- RECOMMENDED FOR ARTICLE

**What happened:** Lovable, an AI-powered app builder, reached $200M ARR in its first year growing 10% month-over-month. Yet Elena Verna (Head of Growth, ex-Miro/Dropbox/Amplitude) says only 30-40% of what she learned in 20 years of growth leadership still applies in the AI era. The core challenge: "Every three months or so, every single AI LLM provider creates a step-function change in what is possible."

**Result:** $200M ARR but must completely recapture PMF every quarter. Traffic swung from +207% growth to near-stagnation within weeks.

**URL (verified):** [CAMINO5: Why Your PMF Now Has a 90-Day Expiration Date](https://www.camino5.com/insights/why-your-product-market-fit-now-has-a-90-day-expiration-date)

**ARGUMENT TEST:** "PMF has a 90-day expiration date. For example, Lovable hit $200M ARR in year one, yet their Head of Growth says they must completely re-earn product-market fit every quarter because AI capabilities shift that fast." -- **PASS: $200M company admitting PMF expires quarterly is shocking proof.**

#### External Case 5B: Chegg -- PMF collapse under AI disruption

**What happened:** Chegg was a $14B education technology company. When ChatGPT launched, students switched to free AI for homework help. CEO admitted on an earnings call that ChatGPT was impacting new customer growth. Stock dropped 49% in a single day (May 2023). By November 2024, Chegg's market cap fell from $14B to $191M -- a 99% decline.

**Result:** Lost 500,000+ subscribers. Market cap: $14B to $191M (99% drop). Revenue declining 13-14% year-over-year.

**URL (verified):** [Gizmodo: Chegg Is On Its Last Legs After ChatGPT](https://gizmodo.com/chegg-is-on-its-last-legs-after-chatgpt-sent-its-stock-down-99-2000522585), [CNBC: Chegg drops 40%](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/05/02/chegg-drops-more-than-40percent-after-saying-chatgpt-is-killing-its-business.html)

**ARGUMENT TEST:** "PMF has an expiration date. For example, Chegg was worth $14 billion. Then ChatGPT launched and students switched to free AI. Two years later, Chegg is worth $191 million -- a 99% drop." -- **PASS: The most dramatic PMF decay case in recent history.**
