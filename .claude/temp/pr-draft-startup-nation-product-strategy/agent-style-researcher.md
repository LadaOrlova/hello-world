## TASK 2: Extracted Stylistic Patterns

### A. STRUCTURAL PATTERNS

**Average article length:** 1,000-1,500 words (confirmed by official style guide). Top-performing articles cluster at 1,200-1,400 words. Book excerpt articles may run slightly longer.

**Headline types (dominant patterns observed across 8 articles):**
- **Numbered list + promise:** "5 Definitive Ways to Drive a High-Growth Business," "7 Scaling Hacks to Unlock Startup Growth," "7 Online Marketing Strategies to Grow Your Business Exponentially"
- **Framework/model name + benefit:** "Know, Go, Grow - The Effective Way to Build Your Company," "Orchestrating Mutual Growth: How B2B Companies Drive Sales and Success"
- **Provocative/contrarian claim:** "Why Startup Advice is the Worst Product-Market Fit on Earth"
- **Social proof + curiosity:** "5 Founders Share the One Piece of Advice They Wish They Knew Before Launching a Business"
- **Key finding:** 6 out of 8 top articles use either a number or a named framework in the headline. Headlines average 8-14 words.

**Paragraph length:** Short -- typically 2-4 sentences per paragraph. Very rarely exceeds 5 sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs are used for emphasis, especially in hooks and transitions.

**Bullet point / list usage:** Heavy. Every article analyzed uses at least one bulleted or numbered list. Lists appear in 2 forms:
1. **Main structural spine** -- the entire article is organized around a numbered list (5 ways, 7 strategies, 7 hacks)
2. **Supporting micro-lists** -- within a section, sub-points are bulleted for scannability

**Subheading frequency:** One subheading (H2 or H3) every 150-250 words. Articles with 5-7 sections are the norm. Subheadings are consistently **bold** and often use the format:
- Imperative verb + noun ("Build Your Infrastructure," "Leverage Technology")
- Number + noun ("Hack #3: Strategic Hiring")
- Named framework step ("Know - Understanding Your Direction")

**Hook format (first 2-3 paragraphs):**
- **Pattern 1 -- Provocative claim:** Open with a bold, slightly contrarian statement that challenges conventional wisdom. Example: "Startup advice sells because it flatters both sides: the giver feels wise and the receiver feels productive. It's a market of comfort, not clarity."
- **Pattern 2 -- Pain/problem statement:** Open by naming a common frustration the reader faces, then pivot to the article's promise. Example: "Growth needs to be measured... but most founders don't know where to start."
- **Pattern 3 -- Data point + question:** Lead with a surprising statistic, then ask a provocative question. Example: "Only 15% of frontline sellers meet customer expectations... What if we were one company?"
- Hooks are typically 1-3 short paragraphs, never more than 100 words before the article's value proposition is clear.

**Conclusion format:**
- Short recap paragraph (2-3 sentences) restating the core takeaway
- Often ends with an empowering call-to-action or motivational closing line
- Pattern: "Growth is an ongoing process that requires hard work, patience, and dedication. There is no quick trick... but there are ways to catapult your business."
- No formal "Conclusion" header -- the ending flows naturally from the final section
- Length: typically 50-100 words

### B. TONAL PATTERNS

**Formality level: 4/10** (where 1 = texting a friend, 10 = academic journal)
- Casual, accessible, but professional. Avoids slang but embraces conversational rhythms.
- AP Style is mandatory per the official guide, which sets a baseline of clean, journalistic prose.

**First person usage:** Mixed.
- Founder/expert pieces use first person freely ("I learned," "my team," "we discovered")
- Editorial/listicle pieces use second person predominantly ("you need to," "your business")
- Third person is used for case studies and data references

**Direct address (second person "you"):** Very heavy. This is the dominant voice across all 8 articles. "You must," "your business," "you'll find" -- readers are spoken to directly in nearly every paragraph.

**Humor:** Light and occasional. Not a comedy publication. Humor appears as:
- Wry observations ("If advice worked, the failure rate of startups wouldn't hover around 90 percent")
- Colloquial phrases ("hockey stick growth," "in the trenches")
- Never sarcastic or self-deprecating to excess

**Expert vs. conversational balance:** 60% conversational / 40% expert. The tone says "I've been there, let me share what works" rather than "Here is the academic framework." Authority comes from experience and data, not credentials and jargon.

### C. CONTENT PATTERNS

**Data density:** Moderate. Top articles include 2-4 data points or statistics per article, but data is always in service of a practical point -- never presented for its own sake.
- Example densities observed:
  - "Building for Growth": revenue growth rates (20%+ for young startups, 10-20% for established), 3:1 LTV/CAC ratio
  - "Orchestrating Mutual Growth": 15% of sellers meet expectations, 14% of senior managers are customer-centric, 2x growth for champions
  - "Why Startup Advice...": 90% startup failure rate
- **Pattern:** One anchor stat in the hook, 1-2 supporting stats in the body, zero stats in the conclusion

**Quote format:** 
- Inline quotes from founders/experts, set in quotation marks within the paragraph body
- Not blockquoted or visually separated
- Format: "Quote text here," says [Name], [Title] at [Company].
- Quote length: 1-2 sentences max -- punchy and quotable, never rambling
- Frequency: 1-3 quotes per article in founder-story pieces; zero in pure listicles

**Case study format:**
- Brief, embedded examples rather than full case studies
- Format: Company name + what they did + result in 2-3 sentences
- Example: "GitLab's experience showing that clear values and autonomous working capabilities matter more than physical proximity"
- Never a standalone section called "Case Study" -- always woven into the advice

**Actionable advice format:**
- Imperative verb + specific action + expected outcome
- "Find the best piece of content on your target topic and create something 10X better"
- "Start and/or keep a journal" -- direct, concrete, immediately implementable
- Lists of steps are numbered; principles are bulleted
- Each piece of advice is followed by 1-2 sentences explaining WHY it works

**Frameworks/models:**
- Articles that name a framework perform well: "Know, Go, Grow," "Triple Fit Strategy," "Startup Growth Pyramid"
- Frameworks are always 3-part (triads dominate): three steps, three pillars, three fits
- Frameworks get their own subheading and are explained in one paragraph each before application

### D. VIRAL / CITABLE PATTERNS

**Conflict type in openings:**
- **Conventional wisdom vs. contrarian truth:** "Startup advice is the worst product-market fit" (challenges the entire advice industry)
- **Ambition vs. reality:** "Growth at any cost" vs. "building lasting value" (Building for Growth article)
- **Effort vs. shortcut illusion:** "There is no quick trick to achieving immediate success" (directly addressing the reader's desire for hacks)
- **Pattern:** The conflict is always framed as "What most people believe" vs. "What actually works"

**Quotable units (social-media-ready one-liners):**
- "Advice may sell, but execution is the only truth that scales."
- "Make no decisions and you're guaranteed to destroy your company."
- "Scaling isn't about growth at any cost, but about building lasting value."
- "Your culture will be the differentiator in the success of your brand."
- **Pattern:** These are declarative, present-tense, absolute statements. Short (under 15 words). Often structured as X vs. Y contrasts.

**Share triggers identified:**
1. **Identity validation:** "You're not failing because you lack advice; you're failing because you lack execution" -- readers share to signal they are action-oriented, not advice-junkies
2. **Numbered utility:** "5 Ways," "7 Hacks" -- readers share because it looks useful and makes the sharer look helpful
3. **Contrarian badge:** "Startup advice is the worst PMF on earth" -- readers share to signal independent thinking
4. **Named framework:** "Know, Go, Grow" or "Triple Fit" -- readers share because naming creates perceived value and makes the sharer look smart
5. **Founder stories:** "5 founders share..." -- social proof creates trust; readers share to align with successful people

## TASK 3: SUPPLEMENTARY STYLE GUIDE for StartupNation.com

> These rules supplement (never override) the media brief. They are derived from analysis of 8 top-performing StartupNation articles and the official 2022 Contributor Style Guide.

---

### Headlines:
- Use 8-14 words maximum
- Include either a NUMBER (3, 5, 7) or a NAMED FRAMEWORK in the headline
- Follow one of three proven formulas:
  - **[Number] [Noun Phrase] to [Desired Outcome]** -- "5 Definitive Ways to Drive a High-Growth Business"
  - **[Named Framework]: [How/What] to [Desired Outcome]** -- "Triple Fit Strategy: How to Build Lasting Customer Relationships"
  - **[Contrarian Claim] + [Domain]** -- "Why Startup Advice is the Worst Product-Market Fit on Earth"
- For our article "Product Strategy: How to Make Your Team Consistently Outperform the Market" -- this aligns with Formula B (Named Framework + How-to + Outcome). It works well for StartupNation.

### Hook (first 100 words):
- Open with ONE of these three patterns:
  1. **Provocative stat + contrarian interpretation** (best for shareability)
  2. **Pain-point question the reader asks themselves daily** (best for engagement)
  3. **Bold declarative statement that challenges the status quo** (best for authority)
- Must include the word "you" or "your" within the first two sentences
- Maximum 3 short paragraphs before the article's promise/roadmap sentence
- The roadmap sentence must preview the article structure: "Here are five ways to..." or "This framework shows how to..."

### Paragraphs:
- Maximum 4 sentences per paragraph; aim for 2-3
- Use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis (1-2 per article, no more)
- No paragraph should exceed 75 words
- Each paragraph should make exactly ONE point

### Subheadings:
- One H2/H3 every 150-250 words (5-7 subheadings for a 1,200-word article)
- Format: **Imperative verb + specific noun** ("Build Your Infrastructure," "Measure What Matters") or **Numbered step** ("Step 3: Validate Your Growth Model")
- Subheadings should be scannable -- a reader who reads ONLY subheadings should understand the article's argument
- Never use question-format subheadings (this is not a Q&A site)

### Tone:
- Formality: 4/10 -- conversational but competent
- Default voice: second person ("you," "your") as the primary address
- First person permitted when sharing direct experience ("I learned," "my team found")
- Read-aloud test: every sentence should sound natural spoken aloud by a founder at a coffee meeting
- AP Style is mandatory (per official guide): no Oxford comma, numerals for 10+, spell out one through nine
- Casual verbiage is acceptable; vulgar/offensive language is not (per official guide)

### Lists:
- Use numbered lists for sequential steps or ranked items
- Use bullet points for non-sequential tips, characteristics, or examples
- Aim for 5-7 items in the main list (the article's structural spine)
- Each list item should have a bold lead-in phrase followed by 1-2 explanatory sentences
- Format: **Bold keyword/phrase:** Explanation sentence. Second sentence if needed.

### Expert Quotes:
- Include 1-3 short quotes from named founders, executives, or researchers
- Maximum 2 sentences per quote
- Format: "Quote text," says [Full Name], [Title] at [Company].
- Quotes should be punchy, opinionated, and quotable on social media
- Never use unnamed sources ("one expert says...") -- always attribute

### Data Points:
- Include 3-5 statistics across the entire article
- Place one anchor stat in the hook (first 100 words)
- Place 1-2 supporting stats in the body sections
- Zero stats in the conclusion
- Always cite the source inline: "according to [Source]" or "(Source, Year)"
- Prefer recent data (2024-2026); avoid anything older than 3 years
- Data should always serve a practical point -- never presented for academic interest

### Conclusion:
- No "Conclusion" or "Final Thoughts" header -- flow naturally from the last section
- 2-3 sentences maximum (50-100 words)
- Structure: Restate core insight in one sentence + Empowering call-to-action or forward-looking statement
- End on a high-energy, action-oriented note -- the reader should feel motivated to DO something
- Never introduce new information in the conclusion

### AVOID:
- Insipid, vague content written for search engines rather than humans (per official guide)
- Overly self-promotional content heavy on PR (per official guide)
- Academic jargon, MBA-speak, or unnecessarily complex frameworks
- Paragraphs longer than 5 sentences
- Opening with a definition ("Product strategy is defined as...")
- Generic advice without specific examples ("focus on your customers")
- Passive voice in advice sections (use "Build X" not "X should be built")
- Blockquotes or pull-quotes (StartupNation uses inline quotes only)
- More than 1,500 words total (official guide upper limit)

### TOP-3 TECHNIQUES from Best-Performing Articles:

1. **The Named Triad Framework**
   The highest-engagement articles give their core idea a catchy 3-part name: "Know, Go, Grow" / "Triple Fit Strategy" / "Startup Growth Pyramid." For our article, the product strategy framework should be distilled into a memorable 3-word or 3-step model that readers can recall and share. Example: "Discover, Decide, Deliver" or whatever the media brief specifies.

2. **The Contrarian Hook**
   The most-shared article in the dataset ("Why Startup Advice is the Worst Product-Market Fit on Earth") opens by attacking a sacred cow. For a product strategy article, the equivalent would be challenging a widely-held belief: e.g., "Most product strategies fail not because of bad ideas, but because teams optimize for the wrong metric." This creates the identity-validation share trigger.

3. **The Numbered Utility Spine**
   6 of 8 top articles use a numbered list as their structural backbone. This is the signature StartupNation format. Even if the article has a more sophisticated framework, it should be presented as "5 Principles" or "3 Steps" -- the number in the title and the numbered sections in the body are the primary scannability and share triggers.

### VIRAL TRIGGERS to Replicate:

1. **Identity Signal** -- Include at least one provocative line that readers will share to signal "I'm a serious operator, not a wannabe." The line should contrast execution with theory, or discipline with hype. Place it in the first 200 words so it appears in social media previews.

2. **Named Framework = Perceived Value** -- Name the methodology. An unnamed method is forgettable advice; a named method is intellectual property. "The [Name] Framework" in the subheading makes the article feel like proprietary knowledge worth bookmarking and sharing.

3. **Founder Proof Points** -- Include 2-3 brief real-company examples (company name + what they did + measurable result). These create credibility and give sharers concrete proof to cite when they recommend the article. Prefer well-known startup brands (Stripe, Notion, GitLab, Airbnb) that the StartupNation audience aspires to emulate.

4. **Quotable Absolutes** -- Write 2-3 declarative, present-tense, under-15-word sentences that work as standalone social media posts. Structure: "[Concept X] is not [common assumption]; it's [contrarian truth]." Example: "Strategy without execution is a PowerPoint. Execution without strategy is chaos."

5. **The "Save This" List** -- If the article contains a checklist, scorecard, or diagnostic tool (even a simple 3-question test), it triggers the "save for later" behavior that boosts algorithmic reach on LinkedIn, Twitter, and bookmarking tools.
