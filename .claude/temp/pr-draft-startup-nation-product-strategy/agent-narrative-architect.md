## TASK 0: Why Now?

Three forces collide in Q2 2026 that make this argument more urgent than it has ever been:

**1. AI slashed the cost of building -- so the bottleneck shifted from execution to selection.**
In 2024, a basic MVP cost $50K-$150K and took months. In 2026, AI vibe-coding tools (Lovable, Replit, Cursor) let a solo founder ship a working product in days for under $1,000. When building is nearly free, the question stops being "Can we build it?" and becomes "Should we build it?" Choosing the wrong customer job used to waste six months of engineering. Now it wastes six months of *market window* -- because three competitors shipped the same wrong thing in the same week. Segment choice is the last remaining competitive moat.

**2. PMF decay accelerated from years to quarters.**
Lovable itself is the proof: $0 to $200M ARR in 12 months, then $400M ARR by February 2026. Elena Verna (Head of Growth) publicly stated they must "fully recapture product-market fit every three months." CAMINO5 coined the term "90-day expiration date" for PMF. If the fastest-growing startup in history treats PMF as perishable, a 20-person SaaS company certainly cannot treat it as a one-time achievement.

**3. The failure data got worse, not better.**
CB Insights' latest analysis of 431 failed VC-backed startups (shutdowns since 2023) shows 43% failed from poor product-market fit -- still the #1 root cause. Median time from last funding round to shutdown: 22 months. Despite all the lean startup methodology, design thinking workshops, and agile transformations of the past decade, nearly half of startups still die from solving the wrong problem for the wrong people. The tools got better. The strategic thinking did not.

**The "Why Now" sentence for the article:**
> "AI made building cheap. That should have made failure rare. Instead, it made bad strategy lethal -- because now the wrong product reaches the wrong market in days, not months."

## TASK 5: Close -- 3 Variants

### Variant A: Decision Reframe

Here's the most common mistake I see: founders treat product strategy as a planning exercise. They build roadmaps, prioritize backlogs, run sprint planning. All of that is execution, not strategy.

Strategy is one decision: which customer jobs will you compete for?

Tomorrow morning, before you open your project management tool, ask yourself three questions:

1. **Who is my customer, really?** Not demographics -- what job are they hiring my product to do?
2. **Is this the most valuable job I could compete for?** Or is there a bigger, more urgent, more profitable job nearby that I'm ignoring?
3. **When did I last validate this?** If the answer is "more than 90 days ago," your fit may already be expiring.

Get those three answers right, and the roadmap writes itself. Get them wrong, and no amount of execution will save you.

*[Word count: 148]*

### Variant B: Provocation

I'll leave you with an uncomfortable question.

If you paused all feature development today and spent one week interviewing 10-12 customers about the *jobs* they're actually hiring your product to do -- would you discover that you've been competing for the wrong job?

In my experience, the answer is yes about 70% of the time. That's not a failure of execution. It's a failure of strategy -- the quiet kind that doesn't show up in dashboards until it's too late.

The fix isn't complicated. It's just uncomfortable. Because it might mean saying no to the customers you already have, in order to say yes to the ones you should.

*[Word count: 113]*

### Variant C: Prediction

Twelve months from now, the startup landscape will look radically different. AI will make building even cheaper. New competitors will appear even faster. PMF windows will shrink even further.

The founders who survive won't be the ones who built the most features. They'll be the ones who made the best decision about which customer jobs to own -- and who kept re-making that decision every quarter.

Start this week. Interview 10 customers. Map their jobs. Find the one you should be competing for. Your roadmap can wait. Your strategy can't.

## TASK 6: Memorable Moment

### The ONE element the reader remembers a week later:

**"InVideo fired half their customers -- and grew from $0 to $25M in six months."**

Why this sticks:

1. **It's counterintuitive.** Every founder's instinct is to serve MORE customers, not fewer. The idea that deliberately *rejecting* a segment leads to explosive growth violates expectations.
2. **It's a story with a number.** Humans remember narratives with concrete outcomes. "$0 to $25M in six months" is specific enough to retell at a dinner conversation.
3. **It's personally threatening.** The reader will involuntarily ask: "Am I serving two conflicting customer types right now?" That question will gnaw at them for days.
4. **It's retellable in one sentence.** The best memorable moments are ones people share. "Did you know InVideo grew from zero to $25M in six months by *firing* half their users?" is a perfect cocktail-party fact.

### Placement
This moment should land in **Section 2 (The Wrong Job Trap)**, approximately 350-450 words into the article. This is the narrative midpoint -- early enough to hook the scanner who skims, late enough that it arrives after the reader understands the framework.

### Reinforcement
Reference it once more in the Close: "Remember InVideo. They didn't build more features. They made a better decision about whose problem to solve." This creates a narrative bookend.

### Budget pressure points
- **Section 1 (300 words)** is the tightest. It must fit the causal chain, the 43% stat, and the three errors. Consider cutting one error (e.g., merge "non-existent jobs" and "unprofitable segments" into a single point about misidentifying the customer) if the section runs long.
- **Section 3 (200 words)** for five mechanics is ~40 words each. This means one sentence per mechanic + one example. No room for explanation -- each mechanic should be a "name + one-line example" format (e.g., "**Kill the job.** Uber killed 'look for change.' AirPods killed 'untangle headphones.'").
- **Mini-case (150 words)** is tight for a full narrative. Use the three-beat structure: situation (1 sentence) -> JTBD insight (2 sentences) -> result (1 sentence with numbers).

### Flex reserve
The 800-1500 range gives ~200 words of flex. If any section naturally runs long during drafting, the budget can absorb it. The most likely section to expand is Section 2 (InVideo case) because stories naturally need more words than arguments. Allow up to 300 words there if needed, and trim Section 1 to 250 to compensate.
