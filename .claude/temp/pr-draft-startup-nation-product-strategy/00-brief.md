# Brief — Product Strategy: How to Make Your Team Consistently Outperform the Market

## Outlet: StartupNation.com
## Word count: 800–1500

## Core idea
Product strategy for small businesses boils down to one decision: which customer jobs will you compete for — and getting this wrong kills more startups than bad execution.

## Theses (verbatim from input file)

### Thesis 1: Product strategy = choosing which customer jobs you compete for. Getting it wrong is the #1 cause of failure.
Everything in a product — value, features, landing pages, acquisition, retention — flows from knowing your customers' jobs. A rigid causal chain exists: Market → Segments → Jobs → Value → Communication → Conversion → Profit. An error at any earlier step breaks everything downstream. The vast majority of startups choose small, difficult, less profitable segments. Three catastrophic errors: (1) Product for non-existent jobs of non-existent people — most common cause of failure. (2) Working on unprofitable segments — a segment several times larger may exist nearby. (3) Spreading resources — features for everyone = value for no one. CB Insights (431 startups): 43% of failures = poor product-market fit.

Cases: ARTDENT (dentistry, +37% revenue), SKAI/Sky (B2B SaaS, +75% revenue), Legal Resources (x13 revenue after 6 years of no profit).

Expert opinions: Bob Moesta ("People don't buy products. They hire them to do a job." 10-12 interviews cover 90% of the market), CB Insights (43% fail from poor PMF, median to shutdown: 22 months), April Dunford (40% of B2B deals lost to status quo, not competitors).

### Thesis 2: Companies compete for the wrong segment, the wrong jobs — and pay for it with missed opportunity.
Companies choose among customers who have many jobs, and the product competes for a small number of them — often not the most optimal. A larger, more solvent segment with higher-frequency jobs may exist nearby — but the founder doesn't know about it. Trying to serve two conflicting jobs simultaneously ("bipolar product problem") tears the product apart: InVideo spent 3 years serving beginners and experts → after JTBD-driven rejection of experts, growth from $0 to $25M in 6 months. Every time a founder builds a feature instead of seeing the full job graph, they incur opportunity cost.

Expert opinions: Bob Moesta (InVideo bipolar product problem), Shreyas Doshi (Opportunity cost thinking > ROI thinking), Nate Stewart (Customer commits = tech debt, Cockroach Labs refused client money for analytics workloads to maintain strategic focus).

### Thesis 3: There are ~80 product strategy mechanics — and each one changes the game.
Key mechanics for the article: (1) Higher-level job — most powerful (Yandex.Rent killed jobs "photograph apartment", "deal with tenants"). (2) Up-market shift — LiFT: check x3 when changing segment. (3) Previous job — Pleada: ROMI 800%→1200%. (4) Next job — cross-sell. (5) Kill the job — Uber killed "look for change", AirPods killed "untangle headphones". Full AURA methodology has ~80 strategies.

### Thesis 4: Case — JetBrains/Kotlin Multiplatform — JTBD in DevTools
JetBrains Kotlin Multiplatform: hundreds of unresolved issues, demographic segmentation (company type, team size) useless. JTBD segmentation revealed 2 key jobs: (1) reducing errors in business logic through shared code, (2) preserving native UI. Competitors focused on "release velocity" — KMP found a different angle. Result: top-3 cross-platform technologies from scratch, +20% satisfaction.

### Thesis 5 (Н1): PMF Decay — product-market fit has a 90-day expiration date.
In the AI era, PMF "expires" every ~90 days. Lovable ($200M ARR) must "fully recapture product-market fit every three months." Customer jobs aren't static either — what "job" a customer hires shifts with technological capabilities. JTBD segment selection is not a one-time decision but a continuous sensing process.
