### Title Patterns
- **Dominant type: Declarative claim + qualifier** -- "What AI can't -- and shouldn't -- do for product managers"; "Precision over hype: A product manager's playbook..."
- **Secondary type: How-to** -- "How to set off on the right product foot in 2025"; "How to succeed as a PM building developer experiences"

- **Key pattern: Titles signal a contrarian or precise stance** rather than generic themes. They promise specificity: a "playbook", a "guide", "four things we learned", a "what + shouldn't" frame.
- **Title case:** Mind the Product uses **sentence case** (lowercase except first word and proper nouns). This is explicit in their editorial guidelines.

### Paragraph Length
- **Modal length:** 3-4 sentences (60-100 words).
- **Opening paragraphs:** Tend to be shorter (2-3 sentences) to create momentum.
- **Deep-analysis paragraphs:** Occasionally reach 5-6 sentences, but never become walls of text.
- **Rule of thumb:** No paragraph exceeds 120 words.

### Subheadings
- **Frequency:** Every 200-400 words (i.e., 4-8 subheadings per article).
- **Style:** Sentence case per editorial policy.
- **Content:** Typically descriptive statements, occasionally questions ("What tools should you master?"), never clickbait.

## PART 3: Tonal Patterns

### Formality Scale
- **Rating: 5-6 out of 10.** Professional but not academic. No jargon for the sake of jargon.
- **Vocabulary:** Industry terms (north star metric, unit economics, guardrails) are used fluently without definitions -- the audience is assumed to be practicing PMs.
- **Sentence structure:** Mix of short declarative sentences and compound sentences. Occasional fragments for emphasis.

### First Person ("I", "we")
- **"I" usage:** 5 of 8 articles use "I". It appears when authors share personal experience, coaching anecdotes, or lessons from their own product work.
- **"We" usage:** 6 of 8 articles. Used to create communal identity ("we as product managers", "we stand at the precipice").
- **Best practice:** The highest-engagement articles (Wanger, Hamlin, Yelland) use "I" for credibility anchors -- brief, specific anecdotes that prove the author has done the work -- and "we" to signal shared challenges.

### Direct Address ("you")
- **Frequency: 7 of 8 articles** use "you" extensively.
- **Function:** Creates immediacy and practical framing ("your CEO", "your roadmap", "your job").
- **The best articles alternate:** "I tried X" (credibility) -> "You face Y" (relevance) -> "We can do Z" (shared solution).

### Humor / Irony
- **Rare but effective when present.** Hamlin's "spaghetti on a wall" metaphor and Wanger's wry observations are the only clear examples.
- **Pattern:** Light irony or vivid metaphor in the hook, then straight-ahead professional tone in the body. MtP does NOT use internet humor, memes, or snark.

### Expert vs. Conversational Tone
- **Blend at 60:40 expert:conversational.**
- Authors write as experienced practitioners sharing what they have learned (not professors lecturing). The implicit posture is "I've been in the trenches, here's what works."
- The most popular articles demonstrate expertise through specificity (naming exact tools, citing exact costs, describing real workflows) rather than through dense theoretical language.

### Use of Personal Experience
- **6 of 8 articles include personal anecdotes or professional observations.**
- **Format:** Brief (1-3 sentences), always in service of a point, never self-aggrandizing.
  - Wanger: "I hosted a webinar..." / "I got caught up in..."
  - Hamlin: "As a coach to product teams, I've also observed..."
  - Yelland: Bio references 14 years of experience, subtly referenced in tone.
- **The best anecdotes are "failure confessions"** -- moments where the author admits they over-automated, missed something, or learned the hard way. This creates trust.

## PART 6: STYLE GUIDE for Writer

### STYLE GUIDE: Mind the Product -- Article "AI Changes the PM Role"

**Title:**
- Use sentence case.
- Pattern: [Contrarian claim or specific angle] + [qualifier for PM audience].
- Avoid generic "AI is transforming PM" framing. The title must promise a SPECIFIC, slightly surprising insight.
- Good models: "What AI can't -- and shouldn't -- do for PMs", "Precision over hype: A PM's playbook for..."
- Aim for 8-14 words.

**Hook (first 2-3 sentences):**
- Open with TENSION or a PARADOX. The reader must feel "wait, that's not what I expected."
- Best pattern: "Everyone says X. But Y is actually true." Then follow with a "this article will show you" bridge sentence.
- Never open with "AI is transforming everything." This is the #1 cliche in MtP's space.
- Strong alternative: Open with a specific, surprising data point or a vivid failure scenario.

**Paragraphs:**
- 3-4 sentences maximum (60-100 words).
- Opening paragraphs: 2-3 sentences for pace.
- Never exceed 120 words in a single paragraph.
- One idea per paragraph. If you introduce a second idea, start a new paragraph.

**Subheadings:**
- Every 200-400 words (target 5-7 subheadings for a 1,800-2,200 word article).
- Sentence case per MtP house style.
- Subheadings should form a readable outline on their own -- a reader who only skims them should get the article's full argument arc.
- Mix declarative statements and occasional questions.

**Tone:**
- Professional-conversational: 5-6/10 on the formality scale.
- Write as a peer practitioner sharing hard-won lessons, not as a professor or consultant marketing services.
- Alternate between "I" (brief personal credibility anchors), "you" (direct reader address), and "we" (shared PM community).
- Assumed reader: a practicing PM with 3-8 years of experience who is already using AI tools. Do NOT explain what GPT is. Do NOT define "product-market fit."

**Voice rhythm:**
- I-You-We rotation: "I discovered X when I..." -> "You've probably seen this in..." -> "We can address this by..."
- Short declarative sentences for emphasis. Longer compound sentences for nuance.
- Occasional one-sentence paragraphs for dramatic punch.

**Format of lists:**
- Use bulleted/numbered lists ONLY for actionable advice, frameworks, and enumerations.
- Keep narrative and argument sections in prose.
- Lists should have 3-6 items (not 10+). Each item: 1-2 sentences.

**Expert quotes:**
- 2-3 quotes per article. One sentence each, attributed inline.
- Cite recognizable PM voices (Cagan, Torres, Perri, Gibson Biddle) or named practitioners at known companies.
- Format: "As [Name], [title at Company], puts it: '[quote].'"
- Use quotes to VALIDATE your point, not to make it for you.

**Data points:**
- Include 2-4 concrete data points / statistics.
- At least one in the first 300 words to establish credibility.
- Weave into prose: "According to [Source], X% of product teams..."
- Prefer surprising or counterintuitive data over confirming-the-obvious stats.
- Name the source (report name, company, researcher).

**Company examples / case studies:**
- Include 2-3 brief (1-3 sentence) company examples.
- Best pattern: show a SPECIFIC failure, surprise cost, or counterintuitive result.
- Name the company. Vague "one company I worked with" is weaker than "when Latitude's AI Dungeon costs spiraled..."
- Never write a multi-paragraph case narrative. Keep it tight.

**Conclusion:**
- Pattern: One-sentence synthesis of the core argument + one forward-looking imperative or provocative question.
- End with something quotable and tweetable. The last sentence should be the one people screenshot.
- Never end with "the future is bright" or "AI and humans will work together." These are MtP cliches.

**PROHIBITED / Anti-patterns:**
- Generic "AI is transforming everything" framing without specifics.
- Academic tone or consultant-speak ("leverage", "paradigm shift", "synergy").
- Advertorial content or product/tool promotion.
- Paragraphs longer than 5 sentences.
- Conclusions that merely restate "AI + humans = good."
- Opening with a dictionary definition.
- Unexplained jargon aimed at non-PM readers (the audience is PMs -- write for them, not for a general business audience).
- Block quotes longer than 2 sentences.
- More than 3 sentences of biographical self-promotion.
- Speculation without grounding ("AI will probably...", "In the future...") -- instead use specific evidence.

### TOP 3 Techniques from the Best Articles

**1. The "Confession Hook" -- open with what went wrong**

The most engaging MtP articles start by admitting a mistake or revealing a counterintuitive failure. This instantly separates the article from generic thought leadership.

Example (Wanger): "I got caught up in the promise of AI-driven efficiency. Then I realized I was automating away the exact human connections that made my products succeed."

How to apply: Open with a moment where AI over-promise hit reality. Show the gap between the hype and what actually happened. This builds trust and creates the tension that powers the rest of the article.

**2. The "Named Framework with a Litmus Test" -- make your advice memorable and testable**

Top-performing articles give their framework a name and provide a single question the reader can use to test it immediately.

Example (Chigrinets): "The Latitude test" -- asking whether heavy usage improves or erodes your margins. One question that encapsulates the entire article's argument.

How to apply: Name the central framework of the article. Then give it a "one-question test" that the reader can apply to their own product on Monday morning. This is the most shareable element of any MtP article.

**3. The "Alternating Proof Stack" -- interleave data, anecdote, and direct advice in a rhythm**

The highest-engagement articles never go more than 300 words without switching evidence types. They rotate between: (a) a data point or stat, (b) a brief personal or company anecdote, and (c) a direct "you should do X" imperative.

Example (Yelland): Marty Cagan quote (authority) -> specific tool list (actionable) -> personal observation about PM identity (narrative) -> direct "here's what to do" tips (imperative). Each section layer-cakes these elements.

How to apply: After drafting, review each 300-word block. Does it contain at least two of the three evidence types (data, anecdote, directive)? If any block is purely theoretical or purely narrative, add the missing element.
