# Agent Optimization — The Next Distribution Layer

SEO → AI SEO → Agent optimization.
We're entering stage 3. Most product teams are still stuck on stage 1.

I've been deep in this shift — rebuilding how agents interact with products, not in theory but through hands-on product strategy work where I see exactly how the distribution layer is changing.

Here's what's happening, and where it's heading.

AI agents are multiplying fast — not just the chat assistants you already use, but procurement agents, integration agents, research agents that autonomously compare, evaluate, and connect tools on behalf of businesses and consumers alike.

Your product can be discovered and "bought" dramatically more often than today. But here's the catch: agents interact with your product differently than humans do.

They still see your landing page. But they prioritize different things. An agent doesn't care about your hero banner or testimonial carousel — it's scanning for structured, machine-readable information: API docs, MCP integration schemas, machine-readable contracts and policies, SDK references. This is the documentation layer that most teams treat as an afterthought, buried three clicks deep. For agents, it's the primary surface they evaluate.

Think about that: the thing you currently treat as "developer docs" is becoming the most important marketing asset for a fast-growing buyer segment.

---

How do agents find you in the first place?

Google isn't going away — but a new discovery layer is forming on top of it. Integration protocols like MCP (Model Context Protocol) and similar standards are emerging as the "USB ports" for AI agents: a universal way to plug into tools, data, and services.

This applies to every type of product. Whether you're selling an API for customer data verification, a food delivery platform, a travel booking engine, or a B2B analytics tool — agents need a standardized way to discover and connect to you. The products that plug into this infrastructure early will compound growth the same way early App Store apps or early Shopify plugins did: first-mover advantage in a brand new distribution layer.

---

Here's where the evolution gets really interesting.

Stage 1 we all know: classic SEO. Optimize for Google, rank on page one, get clicks. This still works and isn't dying.

Stage 2 emerged over the past 2 years: AI SEO. Optimize to get cited by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini. Pew Research has already shown that when AI summaries appear, users click through to sources significantly less. You want to be the answer, not just a link.

Stage 3 is fundamentally different — it's not about appearing in an answer, it's about becoming a transactional step in an agent's workflow.

Today's AI optimization means "get mentioned in ChatGPT's response." Tomorrow's means "get integrated into a procurement agent's execution chain so it can automatically evaluate your specs, negotiate terms, and complete the integration — without a human ever visiting your website."

The difference is profound:
→ Stage 2: You're a citation, a reference. The user might click, might not.
→ Stage 3: You're a transaction. The agent discovers, evaluates, integrates, and transacts — end to end.

---

What does this mean for your product strategy right now?

You don't need to rebuild anything. But you probably need to add a new layer — what I'd call the "agent-readability layer":

→ Structured, machine-readable product specs alongside your human-readable docs
→ Protocol-ready integrations (MCP servers, standardized APIs) so agents can plug in
→ Machine-readable policies — pricing, SLAs, terms that an agent can parse and compare
→ Content designed to be cited and consumed by AI, not just read by humans
→ Measurement infrastructure for "AI referrals" — because you can't optimize what you can't track

The distribution funnel is gaining a new parallel track:

Traditional: link → click → convert
Agent-native: agent → discover → evaluate spec → integrate → transact

Both will coexist. But the second one is growing exponentially.

---

The direction is clear: more agents, more automation, more machine-to-machine discovery. The product teams building their agent-readability layer now won't be scrambling when this becomes mainstream.

What's your take — is your product ready to be "read" by an agent?
