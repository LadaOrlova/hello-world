# How to Find Product-Market Fit Fast — and Pivot the Right Way

## Article Theses / Outline for Hackernoon

---

## Hook (3-4 sentences)

Everybody knows this stuff. There have been countless lectures, blog posts, podcasts about how you need to validate fast, how the #1 startup killer is "no market need," how you should talk to customers and sell before you build. And yet — founders still spend months perfecting their product while procrastinating on sales. They add one more feature, polish the UI, tweak the onboarding. Anything but picking up the phone. Why? Because we fall in love with what we build. And that love is the real problem.

---

## Section 1: The Problem — We Fall In Love With Our Products

**Thesis:** The fatal flaw isn't lack of knowledge. It's emotional attachment. We identify ourselves with our products, and that makes us blind.

Key points:
- Y Combinator calls this "Solution in Search of a Problem" — you have a beautiful MVP and you're desperately trying to find someone to buy it
- When you launch without knowing your segments, you operate in the dark: no sales → no idea why → no actionable feedback
- The real issue: **we identify ourselves with our ideas**. Our product becomes an extension of our ego. Criticism of the product feels like a personal attack.
- When you're attached to your idea, your brain literally ignores information that threatens your beliefs. It's called confirmation bias, and it's running the show.
- The "made a landing page, bought traffic, and it worked" story is survivorship bias — we don't see the 98-99% who did the same and got zero traction

**A recurring pattern I've observed:** Teams skip customer research because "we already know our market." Then they spend 6-12 months building, adding features, polishing — anything to avoid the moment of truth. They eventually pivot anyway — but now with depleted resources and shattered confidence.

The math is brutal: if you have 7 independent risky assumptions, each with a 40% success probability (unrealistically generous), your overall odds are ~3%. With realistic 10-20% odds per assumption? Essentially zero. **Every feature you add without validation is another assumption you're stacking against yourself.**

**What "no market need" actually means:** When researchers say 90% of startups fail because of "no market need," they're not saying your product is bad. They're saying one of two things happened:
1. **You built a solution for a job that doesn't exist** — or exists for too few people to matter. The segment you imagined isn't real, or it's tiny.
2. **The job exists, but your product doesn't do it better** — you're not more efficient at executing the job than existing solutions. No added value = no reason to switch.

Both failures look the same from the outside: nobody buys. But the root causes are completely different, and so are the fixes. That's why understanding jobs — not just problems — is critical.

## Section 2: The Paradigm Shift — Kill, Don't Launch (Introducing RAT)

**Thesis:** Your goal is not to launch a product. Your goal is to kill it as fast and cheaply as possible. This is the core idea behind Risk Assumption Test (RAT).

Back in 2016, [Rik Higham wrote on Hackernoon](https://hackernoon.com/the-mvp-is-dead-long-live-the-rat-233d5d16ab02) that the MVP is dead and we should focus on RAT — Risk Assumption Test. The idea: instead of building a Minimum Viable Product, identify your riskiest assumptions and test them directly.

Key points:
- **Reframe**: you're not launching a product, you're buying knowledge. The question becomes: "Does this set of assumptions have a chance to survive, or is there already something that will kill it?"
- Your idea has a 90%+ chance of being non-viable — you just don't know why yet. The faster you find that reason, the faster you can move to a set of assumptions that actually works.
- This mindset kills emotional attachment. You stop being the "brilliant founder with a vision" and become a "cold-blooded selector" — like a breeder who knows most specimens won't make it.
- **The ego is your enemy**. When you're attached to your idea, you're not a scientist running experiments. You're a lawyer defending your client.

**The Five Core Risky Assumptions (RAT Framework):**
1. **Market**: Is there enough money? Is it growing? Is it regulated?
2. **Segment**: Have we found an economically attractive segment?
3. **Value**: Does the segment buy our product?
4. **Unit Economics**: Do we get target margin per paying customer?
5. **Demand**: Can we create and scale demand?

If ANY of these fails — game over. There's no "good enough on 4 out of 5."

**The Prioritization Formula:**
```
Priority = (Probability of Risk × Cost of Failure) / Cost of Validation
```

**Here's a specific mechanism that consistently produced results:** Define a product's death as "learning that a critical assumption is false" rather than "running out of money." This reframe turns every failed experiment into progress, not defeat.

## Section 3: Jobs Are Primary — What You're Really Competing For

**Thesis:** Everything in product development flows from one root cause: the jobs your customers are trying to accomplish. When you launch a product, you're choosing which jobs to compete for.

Key points:
- **The job (what the customer is trying to accomplish) is the root cause of everything.** Your landing page, your features, your pricing, your sales script — all of it flows from understanding the jobs of your target segments.
- When you sell, you're discovering two things: (1) What jobs exist in the market, and (2) For which jobs can you create additional value that people will pay for.
- Segment by jobs first, not demographics. "Remove hair cheaply" and "remove hair painlessly in premium salon" are different jobs = different segments = different products = different businesses.
- **Problem interviews are not enough.** Why?
  - Problems may be misidentified
  - Problem segment may be too small
  - They may not pay for solutions
  - Habit already formed around current workaround
  - Problem-focus ignores adjacent jobs where the real value lies

**Critical insight:** Our resources are finite. If we try to create value for all jobs of all people, we'll create just a tiny bit for everyone. No segment will say "wow." Focusing means refusing to invest in certain jobs for certain people — so you can crush it for the jobs you do choose.

**One example illustrates how this works in practice:** Twiggy, a startup founded by ex-Russian bank executives, initially built "Excel on steroids" for German businesses. After interviews, they discovered Germans were perfectly happy with Excel. But getting a business loan took an average of 2 years. They pivoted from "better Excel" to "faster access to credit" — completely different market, job, product, and business model. That's what a real pivot looks like — changing the jobs you compete for.

## Section 4: The PMF Gradient — It's Not Binary

**Thesis:** Product-Market Fit is not a yes/no state. It's a gradient with four distinct stages, each requiring different actions.

The Four Stages (framework from Oleg Yakubenkov):

1. **No PMF (Complete Uncertainty)**
   - You have an idea. Maybe an MVP. Zero sales.
   - Focus: Finding the segment. Job-to-be-done is the root cause.
   - Reality: You don't know if segments exist, what jobs they have, how they currently solve them.

2. **Weak PMF (Sporadic Sales)**
   - First sales happen, but they're not repeatable. You don't fully understand what's driving them.
   - Focus: Segment + Value + Unit economics hypothesis.

3. **Strong PMF (Repeatable Sales)**
   - You found segments, refined value, and have working acquisition channels.
   - Focus: Efficiency of acquisition and operations.

4. **Overwhelming PMF ("Great Problem to Have")**
   - Demand is so strong your main worry is not dying from it.
   - Example: Zoom during COVID grew 4x in revenue. They had to close registrations temporarily.

## Section 5: What a Pivot Actually Is

**Thesis:** A pivot is not "trying something new." It's the deliberate change of one or more risky assumptions in your bundle.

Key distinction:
- Twiggy changed: market, job, product, business model — full pivot
- Some teams only change country (Russia → USA) while keeping everything else — still a pivot, but narrower

**The Iteration Pattern:**
Assumption bundle A → doesn't work → change assumptions → sales start → hit ceiling → change assumptions again (channels, pricing, model) → scales → repeat

**Case study: Dima Zaryuta (3 years, 50+ attempts)**
- Started with video consultations for retail employees (Russia). Worked for one client.
- Pivoted to USA. Market was different — companies had fired those employees. No job existed.
- Pivoted to productivity Chrome extensions. 10 power users loved it. Zero revenue.
- Pivoted to AI-powered personalized outreach. Worked for 6 months, then everyone did it.
- Each pivot changed different assumptions. The founder wasn't failing — he was systematically eliminating non-viable paths.

## Section 6: The Risk of Local Optimum — Why JTBD Research Matters

**Thesis:** When you build first and sell second, you risk getting stuck in a local optimum. JTBD research dramatically increases your chances of finding more profitable segments and higher-value jobs.

Key points:
- **Local optimum trap**: You build a product, find some customers, optimize for those customers — and miss the fact that a much more profitable segment exists right next door.
- **Top-down beats bottom-up**: Instead of starting with your solution, start with the market, then find the segment, then validate the value proposition.
- **The Four Stages of Top-Down Discovery**:
  1. **Market Selection**: How much money flows through this job? (Total spend on the Big Job or Core Job)
  2. **Segment Discovery**: AJTBD interviews (30+ for B2C, 25+ for B2B). Find segments. Rank by attractiveness.
  3. **Value Validation**: Solution interviews, sales attempts (iterate in batches of 6 until conversion)
  4. **Demand Testing**: Channel experiments, creative testing, CAC optimization

**In my experience across multiple product teams,** the biggest mistake is treating assumptions as abstract ("there's a market") rather than concrete ("the market of Russian-speaking emigrants to specific European countries is growing"). The devil — and the opportunity — is in the specifics.

**When MVP wins:**
- MVP costs <$5K and you're comfortable iterating 10+ times
- Segments definitely exist, but you're testing whether they'll adopt an unfamiliar product form (e.g., scooters for urban transport)

**When Research wins:**
- MVP costs >$10K
- You don't know if segments exist or what jobs they have
- You need to understand the job graph before deciding what to build

## Section 7: Expert Interviews — The Underutilized Superweapon

**Thesis:** Before deep research, talk to people who already know your market. Their crystallized knowledge can save you months.

Marc Andreessen has emphasized that one of the best things founders can do is talk to people who've already tried and failed in their market. Their hard-won knowledge is often freely available if you just ask.

**Who to interview:**
- **Founders of failed startups in your space** — they're surprisingly willing to talk. Their ego isn't tied to protecting the idea anymore.
- **Sales directors at competitors** — they know which segments buy and why.
- **Marketing directors of adjacent products** — they understand the demand landscape.
- **Product directors at competitors** — they know what works and what doesn't.

**Case study:** A team choosing between EdTech segments (courses, tutoring, colleges, bachelor's, or master's programs) conducted 20+ expert interviews. Changed their market choice 6 times based on feedback. Eventually landed on online master's degrees. Within a year, captured 10% market share with a new product.

**The ROI is off the charts:** Write 20 messages, get 2 calls, get 1 valuable conversation — your entire investment is already recouped.

## Section 8: Sales Are the Ultimate Validation — But Know Your Context

**Thesis:** Sales are the ultimate validation of multiple risky assumptions at once. But they only work if you understand which segment and which job you're validating.

Key points:
- **Sales validate everything at once**: Does the segment exist? Do they have this job? Is the value proposition compelling? Will they pay?
- **But there's a catch**: If you don't know your segment and job, you're selling in the dark. You might get a "no" — but have no idea why, or whether it applies to everyone or just that one prospect.
- **The right question isn't "are people buying?"** — it's "are people in THIS segment with THIS job buying?"

**The iteration pattern for sales validation:**
- Do 6 solution interviews/sales attempts
- No sales? → Reframe value proposition, change pricing, adjust communication
- Still no sales after 5 iterations? → You fundamentally got the segment or job wrong. Go back to research.

**A specific mechanism that works:** When you do solution interviews, don't just pitch. Understand: What job are they trying to accomplish? How do they currently solve it? What would make them switch? Then — and only then — present your offer.

**One case in point:** A founder targeting junior product managers assumed they wanted to "learn to create customer value." After 60 sales calls with zero conversions, he discovered the real job was completely different: junior PMs wanted to "earn respect from engineers." The job he was selling against didn't exist for that segment.

## Section 9: The Guaranteed Way to Kill Your Product

**Thesis:** The more risky assumptions you stack, the closer to zero your probability of success.

The math:
- 7 independent risks, each with 40% success rate (unrealistically generous)
- 0.4^7 = ~3% overall success probability
- With realistic 10% success per assumption: 0.1^7 = 0.00001%

**The antidote:** Before adding any complexity, ask: "What assumption can I remove entirely?"

Examples of what to cut:
- Custom development → no-code
- Multiple product integrations → one integration
- Broad segmentation → narrow focus on one job

**Counterintuitive wisdom:** When the wide solution works, you can later narrow down. But starting narrow and dying is worse than starting simple and expanding.

## Conclusion: The Algorithm Summary

1. **Accept that your idea is probably non-viable** — you just don't know why yet
2. **Recognize your emotional attachment** — it's the real enemy, not lack of knowledge
3. **List all risky assumptions** — be specific, not abstract
4. **Prioritize by: (Probability × Cost) / Validation Cost**
5. **Start with segments and jobs** — this is the root cause of everything else
6. **Use expert interviews** to avoid obvious traps (talk to failed founders, competitors' directors)
7. **Validate value through sales attempts**, not opinion surveys — but know which segment and job you're testing
8. **Watch for local optimum** — JTBD research helps you find better segments, not just optimize for who showed up first
9. **Define pivot as "changing assumption bundle"** — track what you're changing
10. **Reduce risk surface** — cut assumptions wherever possible

**The meta-principle:** You're not building a product. You're buying knowledge about which set of assumptions can survive reality. And you can't buy that knowledge while protecting your ego.
